GST FOR E-COMMERCE SELLERS AND OPERATORS — INDIA AY 2025-26 AND AY 2026-27

This guide covers GST compliance specifically for e-commerce sellers, marketplace operators, and digital commerce platforms under the Central Goods and Services Tax Act, 2017 and allied laws.

---

## Overview of E-Commerce GST Framework

E-commerce in GST terminology covers the supply of goods or services or both through a digital or electronic network. The key players are:

**E-Commerce Operator (ECO):** The owner/operator of the digital platform through which supply is facilitated (e.g., Amazon, Flipkart, Meesho, Swiggy, Zomato, Nykaa).

**E-Commerce Seller/Supplier:** The person who supplies goods or services through the ECO's platform.

These two categories have distinct GST obligations.

---

## Section 52 CGST — Tax Collected at Source (TCS) by E-Commerce Operators

Section 52 of the CGST Act requires E-Commerce Operators to collect Tax Collected at Source (TCS) from the net value of taxable supplies made through their platform.

**TCS Rate:**
- 1% (0.5% CGST + 0.5% SGST/UTGST) on intra-state supplies
- 1% IGST on inter-state supplies

**What Is Subject to TCS:**
- Net value of taxable supplies = Gross value of supplies − Returns
- TCS applies on the consideration collected by the ECO on behalf of the supplier

**Example:**
- Seller lists goods worth ₹1,00,000 on Amazon
- Customer purchases ₹80,000 worth of goods
- Amazon deducts 1% TCS = ₹800
- Amazon remits ₹79,200 to seller (after deducting ₹800 TCS + platform commission)
- Amazon deposits ₹800 as TCS with the government via GSTR-8

**Benefit to Seller:** TCS collected and deposited by the ECO is reflected in the seller's Electronic Cash Ledger and can be used to pay their GST liability. It reduces the cash outflow for the seller.

**GSTR-8:** ECOs must file GSTR-8 (TCS return) monthly by the 10th of the following month.

**Who Is an ECO for This Purpose:** Any person who owns, operates, or manages digital or electronic facility or platform for electronic commerce. This includes both marketplace models and some inventory models.

---

## Mandatory GST Registration for E-Commerce Sellers — Section 24(ix)

One of the most important and frequently misunderstood aspects of e-commerce GST is that e-commerce sellers are required to register for GST regardless of their annual turnover.

**Section 24(ix) CGST:** Persons who supply goods or services through an e-commerce operator (who is required to collect TCS) are compulsorily required to be registered under GST.

**No Threshold Exemption:** Unlike regular businesses (which get a turnover threshold exemption of ₹20 lakh or ₹40 lakh), e-commerce sellers have NO threshold. Even if annual turnover is ₹1 lakh, registration is mandatory.

**Exception for Supplier of Services:**
- Suppliers who supply services exclusively through an ECO are exempt from this mandatory registration if their aggregate turnover is below ₹20 lakh (₹10 lakh in special category states), provided the ECO is required to pay GST on their behalf (Notification No. 65/2017 and its modifications)
- This applies to specific service categories like restaurant services supplied through food delivery platforms (where ECO pays GST) — but this is a limited exception

**Practical Impact:** Any seller on Amazon, Flipkart, Meesho, Nykaa, or similar marketplace must register for GST from Day 1, irrespective of sales volume.

---

## Composition Scheme — NOT Available for E-Commerce Sellers

The Composition Scheme under Section 10 of the CGST Act (which allows small businesses to pay a flat, lower rate of GST and have simplified returns) is NOT available to:
- Persons making inter-state outward supplies
- Persons who supply goods/services through an ECO subject to Section 52 TCS collection

**Consequence:** E-commerce sellers cannot avail of the simpler 1%/5% composition rates. They must register as regular taxpayers and file monthly/quarterly returns.

---

## Input Tax Credit for E-Commerce Sellers

E-commerce sellers registered as regular taxpayers are entitled to claim Input Tax Credit (ITC) on:
- GST paid on goods purchased for resale
- GST paid on capital goods (furniture, equipment, warehouse infrastructure)
- GST paid on services used for business (packing materials, warehousing, logistics paid directly by seller)

**ITC Not Available On:**
- Personal consumption items
- Goods or services for exempt supplies
- Motor vehicles (generally)
- Food and beverages

**Reconciliation Requirement:**
- ITC can only be claimed if the supplier has filed their GSTR-1 and the supply appears in the buyer's GSTR-2B
- Section 16(2)(aa) mandates matching with GSTR-2B — ITC not reflected in GSTR-2B cannot be claimed unless conditions are met

---

## Place of Supply for E-Commerce Transactions

**For Goods (Section 10 IGST Act):**
- Place of supply is the location where goods are delivered to the recipient
- If delivered to a state where the seller is not registered, IGST applies
- If delivered to the same state as seller's registration, CGST + SGST applies

**For Services (Section 12 and 13 IGST Act):**
- Intra-state: Place of supply = location of recipient (if registered) or where service is performed (if unregistered)
- OIDAR services (digital services): Place of supply is location of recipient (see separate section below)

**Intra-State vs Inter-State:**
- Most e-commerce transactions are inter-state (seller in one state, buyer in another) → IGST applies at 1% TCS
- When both seller and buyer are in the same state → CGST + SGST applies

## GST Returns for E-Commerce Sellers

E-commerce sellers (as regular taxpayers) must file:

**GSTR-1 (Outward Supplies):**
- Monthly (if turnover > ₹5 crore): Due by 11th of following month
- Quarterly (QRMP scheme, if turnover ≤ ₹5 crore): Due by 13th of month following quarter
- Must report all B2B sales (with buyer GSTIN) and B2C sales separately

**GSTR-3B (Summary Return and Tax Payment):**
- Monthly (if turnover > ₹5 crore): Due by 20th of following month
- Quarterly (QRMP): Due by 22nd/24th depending on state category
- TCS credit from GSTR-8 (filed by ECO) reflected in electronic cash ledger and can be used here

**GSTR-9 (Annual Return):**
- Due December 31 of following FY
- Reconciles data across all monthly/quarterly returns

**For E-Commerce Operators:**
- GSTR-8 (TCS return): Monthly, by 10th of following month
- GSTR-9B (Annual statement of TCS): By December 31 of following FY

## Marketplace Model vs Inventory Model

**Marketplace Model:**
- ECO acts only as a facilitator/platform
- Title of goods never passes to ECO
- GST liability is on the seller, not the operator
- ECO's GST applies only to its commission/service fee income
- TCS obligations under Section 52 apply to the ECO

**Inventory Model:**
- ECO purchases goods and sells them (like a retailer)
- GST applies to ECO as a supplier — regular tax liability on all sales
- Section 52 TCS not applicable since ECO is the supplier, not a facilitator
- Input tax credit available on purchases

**Hybrid Models:** Some platforms operate partially as marketplace and partially as inventory. Careful analysis is needed for each category of goods sold.

## GST on Delivery Charges, Packaging, and Convenience Fees

**Delivery Charges:**
- Delivery charges are generally taxable at the same rate as the goods being delivered if charged as part of the supply
- If separately contracted delivery services are charged, they may attract 18% GST (transportation services)
- When delivery is a composite supply with goods, the principal supply rate applies to the whole

**Packaging Charges:**
- If packing is part of the supply of goods, packaging is taxed at the same rate as the goods
- Separate packing services provided by a packer may attract 12% or 18% GST

**Convenience Fees/Service Fees Charged by ECO:**
- The ECO charges commission or convenience fee from sellers or buyers
- This is a service and typically attracts 18% GST
- ECOs must charge GST on their platform fee, commission, subscription fees, etc.

**Cancellation Fees and Penalties:**
- Cancellation fees or no-show charges are taxable at 18% GST as service income

## Cross-Border E-Commerce — OIDAR Services

Online Information and Database Access or Retrieval (OIDAR) services are a special category covering digitally supplied services.

**Definition (Section 14 IGST Act):**
Services delivered over the internet or electronic network that are automated and require minimal human intervention:
- Online streaming (Netflix, Spotify)
- Online gaming, e-learning platforms
- Cloud services (SaaS, PaaS, IaaS)
- Online advertising services
- Web hosting
- App store downloads
- Online marketplaces for digital goods

**GST on OIDAR — When Non-Resident Supplier Provides Services to Indian Consumers:**
- Non-resident OIDAR providers supplying to non-registered (B2C) Indian consumers must register for GST in India
- They pay IGST at 18% on the supply
- No TCS mechanism — the foreign company itself must file and pay
- This covers companies like Netflix, Spotify, Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure

**When Indian Business Buys OIDAR from Foreign Provider (Reverse Charge):**
- The Indian recipient (if GST registered) must pay IGST under Reverse Charge Mechanism (RCM) under Section 5(3) IGST
- Rate: same as applicable rate for the service (generally 18%)
- Can claim ITC on RCM paid if for business purposes

## E-Invoicing for E-Commerce

**Current E-Invoicing Threshold:** From August 1, 2023, e-invoicing became mandatory for taxpayers with aggregate turnover exceeding ₹5 crore in any preceding financial year.

**Impact on E-Commerce Sellers:**
- E-commerce sellers with turnover above ₹5 crore must generate e-invoices through the IRP (Invoice Registration Portal) for all B2B invoices
- The Invoice Reference Number (IRN) and QR code must be included on every B2B invoice
- Failure to generate e-invoice for B2B supply = the supply is treated as if made without a tax invoice, with penalty implications

**Exempt from E-Invoicing:**
- B2C invoices (supplies to end consumers)
- Supplies made to SEZ units (under bond/LUT)
- Certain sectors (banking, insurance, etc.)

**Practical Note for Marketplaces:** For marketplace sellers, B2B supplies (to GST-registered businesses) require e-invoicing if their turnover exceeds ₹5 crore. The e-invoice data auto-populates GSTR-1.

## Recent GST Changes Affecting E-Commerce

**Notification for Restaurant Services via ECO (2022):**
- From January 1, 2022, ECOs (like Swiggy, Zomato) are liable to pay GST on restaurant services supplied through their platform
- This reversed the earlier position where individual restaurants paid GST
- ECOs now pay 5% GST on food delivery order value (no ITC available on this supply)
- Individual restaurant suppliers are exempt from GST on supplies through ECOs (but remain liable for dine-in supplies)

**Quick Commerce and Dark Stores:**
- Products sold from dark stores (warehouse models by Blinkit, Zepto, etc.) are treated as inventory model
- GST applies as regular supply — no TCS mechanism applicable since the ECO is the actual supplier

**GSTN Integration with ECOs:**
- GSTN has developed B2B integration APIs that major ECOs use to verify seller GSTIN and file aggregated data
- Sellers should ensure their GSTIN on all platforms is up to date

## Compliance Penalties for E-Commerce

**For ECO failing to collect TCS (Section 52(14)):**
- Penalty equal to TCS amount not collected
- Interest at 18% per annum on the uncollected TCS

**For ECO failing to file GSTR-8:**
- Late fee: ₹200/day (₹100 CGST + ₹100 SGST) subject to ₹5,000 maximum

**For Seller failing to register despite mandatory registration:**
- Penalty: 100% of tax due or ₹10,000 whichever is higher
- Any supply made without registration is treated as a tax evasion offense

**Reconciliation Mismatches:**
- If GSTR-8 filed by ECO does not match GSTR-1 filed by seller, the discrepancy is flagged by GSTN
- GSTN issues notices to either ECO or seller for reconciliation

## Practical Compliance Checklist for E-Commerce Sellers

1. **Register for GST immediately** upon listing on any e-commerce platform — no threshold exemption.

2. **File GSTR-1 on time** — delays result in GSTR-2B issues for your buyers, affecting their ITC claims and your relationship with B2B customers.

3. **Reconcile TCS credit monthly** — Check that TCS deducted by platforms (Amazon, Flipkart, etc.) appears in your GSTR-2B and utilize it against your GSTR-3B liability.

4. **Update GSTIN on all platforms** — Your GSTIN on the selling platform must match your GST registration to avoid reconciliation issues.

5. **Track place of supply** — For sellers registered in one state supplying to customers in multiple states, IGST vs CGST/SGST distinction is important for return filing.

6. **Maintain separate records for returns and cancellations** — The TCS base is net of returns; maintain accurate records to avoid over-payment or under-payment of GST.

7. **E-invoice compliance** — If turnover crosses ₹5 crore, implement e-invoicing immediately for all B2B transactions.
