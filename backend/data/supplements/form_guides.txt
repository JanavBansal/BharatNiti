COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE TO IMPORTANT INCOME TAX FORMS — AY 2025-26 & AY 2026-27
=============================================================================
Income Tax Act, 1961 | CBDT Forms | Filing Procedures and Deadlines
Assessment Year 2025-26 (FY 2024-25) and AY 2026-27 (FY 2025-26)

This guide covers all important income tax forms, their purpose, applicability, filing deadlines, and critical compliance requirements. Understanding the correct form to use is fundamental to accurate tax compliance.

---

## Form 15G and Form 15H — Self-Declaration to Avoid TDS on Interest Income

Purpose: Self-declaration forms submitted to the payer (bank, NBFC, cooperative society, etc.) requesting them not to deduct TDS on interest income, on the basis that the taxpayer's income does not exceed the taxable limit.

Form 15G — For Individuals and HUFs below 60 years:
- Submitted by: Resident individuals (below 60 years), HUFs, trusts (other than companies and firms)
- Condition 1: The estimated total income of the year from all sources should be below the basic exemption limit (₹2.5 lakh under old regime; ₹3 lakh under new regime)
- Condition 2: The aggregate of interest income from all sources for which 15G is being submitted must not exceed the basic exemption limit
- Both conditions must be satisfied simultaneously. If either condition fails, Form 15G is not valid.

Form 15H — For Senior Citizens (age 60 years and above):
- Submitted by: Resident individuals aged 60 years or more
- Condition: Only one condition — the estimated total income from all sources for the year should be below the taxable limit (taking into account the higher exemption limit of ₹3 lakh for senior citizens or ₹5 lakh for very senior citizens above 80 years)
- Form 15H does NOT have the second condition applicable to 15G (the aggregate interest income ceiling does not apply for 15H)

TDS applicable on interest income (without 15G/15H):
- Section 194A: TDS at 10% on FD interest exceeding ₹40,000 per year per bank (₹50,000 for senior citizens)
- Section 194: TDS on dividends exceeding ₹5,000

When to submit: Submit at the beginning of each financial year. The form is valid for one financial year only and must be re-submitted each year.

How to submit: Most banks allow online submission through net banking or the bank's website. Physical submission at the branch is also accepted.

PAN mandatory: If PAN is not furnished, TDS is deducted at 20% (under Section 206AA), irrespective of 15G/15H submission.

Important: Submitting a false 15G/15H declaration to avoid TDS constitutes a false statement under Section 277 of the Income Tax Act, punishable with imprisonment of 6 months to 7 years.

## Form 10-IEA — Opting Out of New Tax Regime for Business Income Taxpayers

Purpose: Formal declaration by a taxpayer with business or professional income to opt out of the new tax regime under Section 115BAC and choose the old tax regime.

Applicable to: Individuals, HUFs, AOPs (other than co-operative societies), BOIs, and artificial juridical persons who have income from business or profession.

NOT required for: Salaried taxpayers without any business income — they can choose their regime at the time of filing ITR without Form 10-IEA.

Filing deadline: Must be filed on or before the due date for filing the ITR for the relevant assessment year (typically July 31 for non-audit cases; October 31 for audit cases).

Key rules:
- A taxpayer who files Form 10-IEA opts out of the new tax regime for that year.
- Once the taxpayer wishes to RETURN to the new tax regime, they must withdraw Form 10-IEA — but this option to return to the new regime can be exercised ONLY ONCE in a lifetime.
- After switching back to the new regime, they cannot again opt out to the old regime.

Practical consideration: Business income taxpayers who benefit significantly from deductions like 80C, 80D, HRA, business depreciation at enhanced rates, etc., and whose deductions exceed the benefit from the new regime's lower tax rates, should file Form 10-IEA each year.

Filing platform: Form 10-IEA is filed online on the income tax e-filing portal (www.incometax.gov.in) under "Forms" — it must be submitted before the ITR is filed.

## Form 12BB — Investment Declaration to Employer for TDS Computation

Purpose: A statement submitted by an employee to their employer declaring all tax-saving investments, deductions, and exemptions they intend to claim during the financial year. The employer uses this information to compute monthly TDS on salary.

Content of Form 12BB:
1. HRA (House Rent Allowance): Rent paid, landlord name, address, PAN of landlord (if rent exceeds ₹1 lakh per year)
2. LTC/LTA: Amount of Leave Travel Allowance claimed with supporting details
3. Home loan interest (Section 24(b)): Name, address, and PAN of lender; interest amount
4. Chapter VI-A deductions: All investments claimed under 80C, 80CCC, 80CCD, 80D, 80E, 80G, 80GG, 80TTA, etc.
5. Any other income or deduction

Filing frequency: Typically submitted at the beginning of the financial year (April) with estimated amounts. Final proof of actual investments must be submitted by January/February (as per employer's deadline for that year) for the employer to recompute TDS and make necessary adjustments in the remaining salary installments.

Employer's TDS computation: Based on Form 12BB, the employer computes projected tax liability, divides by 12 months, and deducts TDS accordingly from each month's salary.

Non-submission or incorrect submission: If Form 12BB is not submitted, the employer deducts TDS without considering any deductions (except statutory ones), leading to excess TDS. The taxpayer can claim a refund while filing their ITR, but it is better to submit Form 12BB accurately to have correct monthly TDS.

## Form 26AS — Annual Tax Statement

Purpose: Form 26AS is a consolidated annual tax statement reflecting all taxes paid on behalf of the taxpayer (TDS, TCS, advance tax, self-assessment tax) as recorded in the Income Tax Department's database.

Contents of Form 26AS:
- Part A: TDS deducted by employers, banks, and other deductors (with TAN of deductor, amount deducted, amount deposited)
- Part B: TCS collected by sellers, e-commerce operators, etc.
- Part C: Advance tax and self-assessment tax paid by the taxpayer (with Challan numbers)
- Part D: Demand/refund details
- Part E: AIR (Annual Information Returns) / SFT high-value transactions
- Part F: TDS under Section 194-IA (property transactions)

Significance in ITR filing: The income tax portal's ITR pre-fill auto-populates TDS credits from Form 26AS. Any TDS not reflecting in Form 26AS cannot be claimed as credit unless the AO verifies it through other means. If there is a mismatch between TDS as shown in Form 26AS and what the taxpayer declares, the automated system under Section 143(1) will flag the discrepancy.

Where to access: www.incometax.gov.in → "e-File" → "Income Tax Returns" → "View Form 26AS" (redirects to TRACES portal).

Note: Form 26AS is being supplemented by AIS (Annual Information Statement), which is more comprehensive. Taxpayers should review both.

## AIS (Annual Information Statement) — Comprehensive Financial Statement

Purpose: AIS is a more comprehensive financial information statement than Form 26AS. It was introduced under Section 285BB and Rule 114-I and provides a 360-degree view of all financial transactions of a taxpayer.

Available at: www.incometax.gov.in → "Services" → "Annual Information Statement (AIS)"

Information captured in AIS (from multiple reporting entities):

1. TDS/TCS information (similar to Form 26AS)
2. SFT (Statement of Financial Transactions) data:
   - Interest on savings accounts and FDs (reported by banks)
   - Dividend income (reported by companies/depositories)
   - Mutual fund purchase/redemption (reported by RTAs)
   - Securities transactions (reported by depositories — CDSL/NSDL)
   - Real estate transactions (reported by sub-registrars)
3. Foreign remittance (from Authorized Dealer banks)
4. Foreign travel and credit card information
5. GST turnover (from GSTN)
6. Off-market securities transfers

Taxpayer Information Summary (TIS): A summary version of AIS reflecting the processed amounts after incorporating taxpayer feedback. The ITR pre-fill is based on TIS.

Taxpayer feedback mechanism: If any information in AIS is incorrect, duplicate, or not relating to the taxpayer, they can submit feedback within AIS:
- Information is correct
- Income is not taxable
- Income is already reported under other head
- Information is duplicate/already included in another record
- Information relates to another person
- Information is denoted in wrong units

Why AIS matters: The Department uses AIS data to identify potential income that is not declared in ITR. Mismatches between AIS and ITR trigger automated notices under Section 143(1)(a).

## Form 16 — TDS Certificate from Employer (Salary TDS)

Purpose: The TDS certificate issued by an employer to their employee for TDS deducted on salary income under Section 192.

Two parts of Form 16:
- Part A: Generated from TRACES (TDS Reconciliation Analysis and Correction Enabling System). Contains: Name and PAN of employer and employee, TAN of employer, assessment year, summary of TDS deducted and deposited quarter-wise.
- Part B: Prepared by the employer (often with help of payroll software). Contains: Detailed computation of salary income — all allowances (taxable and exempt), deductions claimed, net taxable income, and tax computation.

Who issues Form 16: Employers who have deducted TDS on salary exceeding ₹2.5 lakh during the financial year must issue Form 16. However, all employers should issue it even if TDS is below threshold.

Due date for issuance: June 15 following the end of the financial year (e.g., Form 16 for FY 2024-25 must be issued by June 15, 2025).

Usage for ITR filing: Employees use Form 16 (Part B in particular) to fill in salary details in ITR. The employer's computation in Form 16 may be used as-is, or the employee may choose their own regime if different from what the employer assumed.

Multiple employers: If an employee changes jobs in a financial year, they receive Form 16 from each employer. Both Form 16s must be reconciled and the total salary, TDS, and tax computation must reflect income from all employers combined.

## Form 16A — TDS Certificate for Non-Salary Income

Purpose: TDS certificate issued by the deductor for TDS deducted on non-salary payments.

Common scenarios for Form 16A:
- Section 194A: TDS on interest income (FDs, recurring deposits) — issued by banks
- Section 194J: TDS on professional/technical fees — issued by companies paying professional fees
- Section 194H: TDS on commission and brokerage
- Section 194D: TDS on insurance commission
- Section 194C: TDS on contractual payments (if applicable)
- Section 194-IB: TDS by tenant on rent exceeding ₹50,000/month

Form 16A is downloaded from TRACES by the deductor and must be issued to the deductee within 15 days of the due date for filing TDS return. For quarterly TDS returns, this is typically within 15 days after the end of each quarter.

How to use in ITR: Report the income (interest, professional fees, etc.) under the appropriate head in ITR, and claim TDS credit based on Form 16A. Always cross-verify Form 16A data with Form 26AS — they should match.

## Form 3CA, Form 3CB, and Form 3CD — Tax Audit Report

Applicability (Section 44AB of Income Tax Act):
Tax audit is mandatory if:
- Business turnover exceeds ₹1 crore (or ₹10 crore if 95%+ transactions are digital — cash receipts and payments each not exceeding 5%)
- Professional receipts exceed ₹50 lakh
- A person who has opted for presumptive taxation under Section 44AD but declares income lower than the prescribed rate (8%/6%)

Forms:
- Form 3CA: Used by persons who are already required to get their accounts audited under any other law (e.g., companies under the Companies Act)
- Form 3CB: Used by persons whose accounts are audited solely under Section 44AB of the Income Tax Act (not required under any other law)
- Form 3CD: The annexure (statement of particulars) accompanying either 3CA or 3CB. Contains detailed information about the taxpayer's income, payments to related parties, depreciation, deductions claimed, loans, tax treatment of various items, etc.

Filing: The tax audit report (3CA/3CB + 3CD) must be filed by the CA on the income tax portal and accepted by the taxpayer. Due date: October 31 of the assessment year (for businesses) or November 30 (for cases with transfer pricing requirements).

## Form 15CA and Form 15CB — Foreign Remittance Declarations

Purpose: Forms required for most foreign remittances out of India to ensure TDS compliance under Section 195.

Form 15CB: A certificate by a Chartered Accountant certifying:
- The nature and details of the remittance
- Applicable tax treaty (DTAA) provisions
- Tax deductible under Section 195 (if any)
- Whether TDS has been deducted or whether the payment is exempt

Form 15CA: A declaration by the remitter (payer) to be filed before the remittance:
- Part A: For remittances below ₹5 lakh in a financial year (no 15CB required)
- Part B: For remittances specifically covered by CBDT Rule 37BB exemption list (no 15CB required)
- Part C: For remittances above ₹5 lakh where a 15CB is required (most common)
- Part D: For remittances not taxable in India under DTAA or otherwise

Filing process: Form 15CB is prepared and digitally signed by the CA. Form 15CA is then filed online on the income tax portal by the remitter (referencing the 15CB). The acknowledged Form 15CA is submitted to the authorized dealer bank before the bank processes the foreign remittance.

Exemptions: Rule 37BB lists 33 categories of payments not requiring 15CA/15CB (e.g., import of goods, salary paid to NRI employees within India, certain recurring transfers).

## ITR Forms — Which Form to Use

Choosing the correct ITR form is critical. Filing the wrong form results in the return being treated as defective under Section 139(9).

ITR-1 (Sahaj):
- Eligible taxpayers: Resident individuals ONLY (not HUF, not NRI)
- Income sources: Salary/pension, ONE house property (no brought-forward loss), other sources (interest, dividends) — EXCLUDING winnings from lottery, races
- Conditions: Total income must be below ₹50 lakh; no capital gains of any type; no directorship in a company; no unlisted equity shares; no foreign income or foreign assets; agricultural income up to ₹5,000

ITR-2:
- Eligible taxpayers: Individuals and HUFs NOT having business or professional income
- Covers: Salary/pension, multiple house properties, capital gains (STCG, LTCG from shares, mutual funds, property), foreign income, foreign assets (Schedule FA), lottery income, directorship in company, unlisted equity shares
- Use ITR-2 if you have ANY capital gains, even if small

ITR-3:
- Eligible taxpayers: Individuals and HUFs with income from business or profession (including F&O trading, intraday trading, freelancing with business income, professional income not under presumptive)
- Includes: All heads of income (salary, business, capital gains, house property, other sources)

ITR-4 (Sugam):
- Eligible taxpayers: Individuals, HUFs, and firms (other than LLP) opting for presumptive taxation
- Applicable sections: Section 44AD (business up to ₹3 crore), Section 44ADA (professionals up to ₹75 lakh), Section 44AE (transport operators)
- Cannot be used if: Total income exceeds ₹50 lakh, you have capital gains, foreign income/assets, or are a director in a company with unlisted equity shares

ITR-5:
- Firms (including LLP), AOPs, BOIs, local authority, cooperative societies, artificial juridical persons

ITR-6:
- Companies (other than companies claiming Section 11 exemption — charitable trust companies use ITR-7)

ITR-7:
- Persons including companies required to furnish return under Section 139(4A), 139(4B), 139(4C), or 139(4D) — trusts, political parties, research institutions, etc.

## Form 67 — Claiming Foreign Tax Credit (FTC)

Purpose: Used by Indian residents to claim credit for taxes paid in foreign countries on income earned abroad, under Section 90/91 of the Income Tax Act and Rule 128.

When required: When an Indian resident has earned income in a foreign country, paid tax there, and wants to avoid double taxation by claiming credit of the foreign tax against Indian tax liability.

Applicability: Available under Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAA) if India has a tax treaty with that country; or under Section 91 (unilateral relief) if no DTAA exists.

Filing deadline: Form 67 MUST be filed on or before the due date for filing the ITR for that year. If filed after the due date, Foreign Tax Credit may not be allowed (as held by several ITAT benches, though some benches have taken a liberal view).

Content of Form 67:
- Country from which FTC is claimed
- Nature of income
- Amount of income (in foreign currency and INR)
- Tax paid in foreign country (in foreign currency and INR)
- Amount of FTC claimed
- Supporting certificate from foreign tax authority or a self-certified statement of taxes paid

Computation of FTC: FTC is limited to the lower of (a) tax paid in the foreign country or (b) Indian tax attributable to the foreign income. It is computed proportionately if multiple countries are involved.

Common example: An NRI who returns to India and receives US-source dividends or freelance income — they can claim credit for the US withholding tax paid, against Indian income tax computed on the same income.

## Form 10BA — Declaration for Rent Paid (Section 80GG)

Purpose: A self-declaration form filed by taxpayers claiming deduction under Section 80GG for rent paid when they do not receive HRA.

Who files Form 10BA: Self-employed persons, freelancers, or salaried employees who do not receive HRA but pay rent for their accommodation.

Content: Declaration that:
- The taxpayer does not receive HRA
- The taxpayer, spouse, minor child, or HUF (of which they are a member) does not own any residential accommodation at the place of employment/self-employment
- The taxpayer does not own any self-occupied property at any other place

Filing: Form 10BA is filed online on the income tax portal before or at the time of filing the ITR. Without filing Form 10BA, Section 80GG deduction is NOT allowable.

Section 80GG deduction amount: Least of:
- ₹5,000 per month (₹60,000 per year)
- 25% of total income (adjusted — before this deduction and certain other deductions)
- Rent paid minus 10% of total income (adjusted)

Important: This deduction is available only in the OLD tax regime. It is not available in the new tax regime under Section 115BAC.

## Challan 280 — Payment of Income Tax (Self-Assessment, Advance Tax, Regular Assessment)

Purpose: The standard challan for payment of income tax by individuals and non-corporate taxpayers.

Types of payment under Challan 280:
- Code 100: Advance tax
- Code 102: Surtax (rare)
- Code 106: Tax on distributed profits (corporates, not individuals)
- Code 107: Tax on distributed income (buy-back, not individuals)
- Code 300: Self-assessment tax (tax paid when filing ITR)
- Code 400: Tax on regular assessment (tax paid after receiving demand notice)

How to pay: Online through the income tax portal (www.incometax.gov.in) using net banking, debit card, or UPI. Payment can also be made at authorized bank branches (physical challan).

Critical requirement: Always verify that:
- The correct Assessment Year is selected (AY 2025-26 for income earned in FY 2024-25)
- The correct type of payment is selected (advance tax = 100; self-assessment = 300)
- PAN is correctly entered
- Tax credit reflects in Form 26AS within 3-5 working days

Incorrect AY on challan: If tax is paid under wrong AY, it does not automatically credit against the correct year's liability. Apply to the AO for rectification under Section 154 with supporting challan.
