SALARY RESTRUCTURING FOR TAX EFFICIENCY — AY 2025-26 AND AY 2026-27
Indian Income Tax Act — Practical Guide for Employees and HR Professionals

## Overview of Salary Restructuring

Salary restructuring involves reorganizing the components of a Cost to Company (CTC) package to maximize take-home pay by legally minimizing tax liability. Under the Income Tax Act, 1961, several components of salary enjoy full or partial exemptions, while others are taxable. By optimizing the mix of taxable and exempt components, an employee can significantly reduce their tax outgo without changing the total CTC.

Salary restructuring is particularly important for employees who choose the Old Tax Regime (OTR), where deductions and exemptions are permitted. Under the New Tax Regime (NTR) introduced by Section 115BAC, most exemptions and deductions are not available, making the old regime preferable for those who can effectively use these provisions.

This guide covers each component available for restructuring with the relevant legal provisions, limits, and practical examples for AY 2025-26 (FY 2024-25) and AY 2026-27 (FY 2025-26).

## House Rent Allowance (HRA) — Section 10(13A)

HRA is one of the most powerful salary components for tax saving. Under Section 10(13A) read with Rule 2A of the Income Tax Rules, the exempt portion of HRA is the MINIMUM of the following three amounts:

1. Actual HRA received from employer
2. Rent paid minus 10% of Basic Salary (Basic + DA)
3. 50% of Basic Salary for metro cities / 40% for non-metro cities

Metro cities for this purpose are: Delhi, Mumbai, Kolkata, and Chennai. All other cities — including Bangalore, Hyderabad, Pune, Ahmedabad — are treated as non-metro for HRA calculation, which means only 40% of basic salary is the ceiling.

Example: If Basic Salary = Rs. 8,00,000 per year, HRA received = Rs. 3,60,000, Rent paid = Rs. 3,00,000 (in Bangalore, non-metro):
- Actual HRA = Rs. 3,60,000
- Rent paid minus 10% of Basic = 3,00,000 - 80,000 = Rs. 2,20,000
- 40% of Basic = Rs. 3,20,000
Exempt HRA = Minimum of three = Rs. 2,20,000

Key points for HRA optimization:
- HRA should ideally be 40-50% of basic salary
- Rent receipts and rental agreement must be maintained as proof
- If annual rent exceeds Rs. 1,00,000, landlord's PAN must be submitted
- HRA exemption is available even if the employee lives with parents — rent can be paid to parents, but parents must declare it as rental income
- Employees in their own homes receive no HRA exemption — the HRA amount becomes fully taxable
- Joint ownership of rented property is not allowed for claiming HRA

Under the New Tax Regime, HRA exemption under Section 10(13A) is NOT available. Employees on NTR receive HRA as fully taxable income.

## Leave Travel Allowance (LTA) — Section 10(5)

Under Section 10(5) read with Rule 6, LTA is exempt for travel within India for self and family (spouse, children, dependent parents, siblings). Key provisions:

- Exemption covers economy class air travel or AC first class rail/bus travel
- Maximum two journeys in a block of four calendar years are exempt
- Current block (2022-2025): two claims can be made
- Next block (2026-2029): two more claims available
- Shortest route principle applies — if actual travel uses a longer route, only the cost of the shortest route is exempt
- Amount spent on hotel, food, or sightseeing is NOT exempt — only travel costs
- LTA can be carried forward: if one journey is not claimed in a block, it can be availed in the first year of the next block (making it 3 claims in that first year)

Optimal LTA structure: Include Rs. 30,000-50,000 in CTC as LTA. The employee submits travel bills once every two years, saving tax on the full amount at their applicable slab rate.

LTA is NOT available under the New Tax Regime.

## Food Coupons and Meal Vouchers — Section 17(2)(viii) read with Rule 3(7)(iii)

Meal vouchers provided by employers (such as Sodexo, Ticket Restaurant) are exempt up to Rs. 50 per meal. Since most employees work approximately 22 days per month with 2 meals per day:

Maximum monthly exemption = 22 days × 2 meals × Rs. 50 = Rs. 2,200 per month
Annual exemption = Rs. 26,400 per year

This is a small but meaningful saving. The meal vouchers must be for actual meals and should not be encashable as cash.

Note: Under Budget 2023 clarifications, this exemption is available under both the Old and New Tax Regimes as it falls under perquisite rules rather than being an exemption under Section 10.

## NPS Employer Contribution — Section 80CCD(2)

This is arguably the most powerful tax-saving component available that works under BOTH the Old and New Tax Regimes.

Under Section 80CCD(2):
- Government employees: Employer can contribute up to 14% of Basic + DA, fully deductible
- Private sector employees: Employer can contribute up to 10% of Basic + DA, fully deductible
- There is NO upper rupee limit on this deduction — unlike other deductions, 10% of a Rs. 50L basic salary is fully deductible
- This deduction is OVER AND ABOVE the Rs. 1.5L limit of Section 80C

For a private sector employee with Basic Salary = Rs. 15,00,000:
Employer NPS contribution at 10% = Rs. 1,50,000
This Rs. 1,50,000 is NOT part of the 80C limit — it is an independent deduction

For a government employee with Basic = Rs. 15,00,000:
Employer NPS contribution at 14% = Rs. 2,10,000 — fully deductible

The employee's own contribution of up to Rs. 50,000 under Section 80CCD(1B) is ADDITIONAL to 80C and is only available under the Old Regime.

NPS employer contribution is the ONLY deduction that is beneficial under both Old and New Tax Regimes. Employees should strongly negotiate for maximum employer NPS contribution in their CTC restructuring.

Tax saved by adding Rs. 1.5L NPS employer contribution for someone in 30% bracket = Rs. 45,000 (plus surcharge if applicable).

## Car Lease Through Employer (Company Car Policy)

When an employer provides a car on lease for both official and personal use, the perquisite value is:
- For engine capacity up to 1600 cc: Rs. 1,800 per month (Rs. 21,600/year) if employer bears running/maintenance costs; Rs. 600 per month (Rs. 7,200/year) if employee bears costs
- For engine capacity above 1600 cc: Rs. 2,400 per month (Rs. 28,800/year) if employer bears costs; Rs. 900 per month if employee bears costs

Since actual car costs (EMI, insurance, fuel, maintenance) can easily exceed Rs. 5-10L per year, declaring only Rs. 21,600-28,800 as perquisite creates massive tax savings.

Example: Employee in 30% bracket, car EMI + costs = Rs. 8L per year
- If employee buys car personally: No deduction, full Rs. 8L from post-tax income
- If employer provides car lease within CTC: Perquisite = Rs. 28,800, tax paid = Rs. 8,640
- Tax saving = approximately Rs. 2,10,000+ in 30% bracket

Note: The car should genuinely be used for official purposes partially. Proper documentation of official usage is essential.

## Leave Encashment — Section 10(10AA)

Under Section 10(10AA):
- Government employees: Leave encashment on retirement/resignation is fully exempt
- Private sector employees: Exempt up to the LEAST of:
  a) Actual leave encashment received
  b) 10 months' average salary (last 10 months' average)
  c) Cash equivalent of unavailed leave (max 30 days per year of service)
  d) Rs. 25,00,000 (enhanced from Rs. 3,00,000 by a notification in 2023)

The limit of Rs. 25,00,000 is a lifetime limit across all employers. Leave encashment received during employment (not on retirement/resignation) is fully taxable.

## Reimbursements — Fully Exempt for Official Use

Several reimbursements are fully exempt from tax if incurred for official purposes:

Telephone and Internet Reimbursement: No specific limit; expenses for official communication are fully exempt under Section 17(2) read with Rule 3. Companies typically reimburse Rs. 1,000-2,000 per month.

Books, Periodicals, and Newspapers: Expenses on books, journals, and newspapers related to the job are fully exempt when reimbursed. Companies can reimburse Rs. 500-1,000 per month.

Education and Training: Course fees, seminar costs, and professional development expenses paid or reimbursed by the employer are fully exempt.

Uniform Allowance: Expense on uniforms required for duty is exempt. This applies to actual uniforms, not regular clothing.

Medical Reimbursement: Note that since FY 2018-19, the medical reimbursement exemption of Rs. 15,000 has been replaced by the standard deduction. However, employer-provided medical facilities (cashless hospitalization) and medical insurance premiums paid by employer remain tax-free as perquisites.

Driver's Salary: If a car is provided by the employer with a driver, the driver's salary is considered a perquisite at Rs. 900 per month (Rs. 10,800/year), creating significant savings.

## Standard Deduction — Rs. 75,000 (New Regime, AY 2025-26)

From AY 2025-26 (FY 2024-25), the standard deduction under the New Tax Regime has been enhanced to Rs. 75,000 (from the earlier Rs. 50,000). Under the Old Tax Regime, the standard deduction remains Rs. 50,000.

This deduction is available to all salaried employees and pensioners automatically, without any proof or documentation. No restructuring is needed — it is applied by default.

Under the Old Tax Regime, the standard deduction of Rs. 50,000 is also available.

## Optimal Salary Structures — Examples

### Example 1: Rs. 15 Lakh CTC (Old Regime)

Basic Salary: Rs. 5,00,000 (33% of CTC)
HRA: Rs. 2,50,000 (50% of basic, assuming metro city)
LTA: Rs. 30,000
Special Allowance: Rs. 2,76,000
NPS Employer Contribution (10% of basic): Rs. 50,000
Performance Bonus: Rs. 2,50,000
Meal Vouchers: Rs. 26,400
Phone/Internet Reimbursement: Rs. 18,000
Books Allowance: Rs. 6,000
Provident Fund (employer 12%): Rs. 60,000 (already in CTC)
Total CTC: ~Rs. 15,00,000

Tax Calculation (assuming rent paid = Rs. 18,000/month, metro city):
Gross Salary: 15,00,000
Less: Standard Deduction: (50,000)
Less: HRA Exemption: ~(1,40,000) [minimum of actual HRA, rent-10%, 50% basic]
Less: LTA (1 journey): (30,000)
Taxable Salary: ~13,80,000
Less: 80C (PF + ELSS): (1,50,000)
Less: 80CCD(1B) NPS: (50,000)
Less: 80D Medical Insurance: (25,000)
Taxable Income: ~11,55,000
Tax: approximately Rs. 1,50,000 + cess

### Example 2: Rs. 25 Lakh CTC (Old Regime)

Basic Salary: Rs. 8,00,000
HRA: Rs. 4,00,000 (50% of basic, metro)
LTA: Rs. 50,000
NPS Employer Contribution (10%): Rs. 80,000
Performance Bonus: Rs. 5,00,000
Car Lease Perquisite (all actual car costs ~Rs. 3L): only Rs. 28,800 taxable
Meal Vouchers: Rs. 26,400
Other Allowances: Rs. 3,00,000
PF Employer: Rs. 96,000

With appropriate deductions (HRA, 80C, 80CCD(2), 80D), taxable income can be brought below Rs. 15L, saving significant tax vs. unstructured salary.

### Example 3: Rs. 50 Lakh CTC (Old Regime)

Basic: Rs. 16,00,000
HRA: Rs. 8,00,000 (50% of basic, metro)
NPS Employer (10%): Rs. 1,60,000 — independent deduction, saves Rs. 48,000 in 30% bracket
Car Lease: Rs. 5,00,000 actual cost, perquisite only Rs. 28,800
LTA: Rs. 75,000
Performance Bonus: Rs. 10,00,000
PF: Rs. 1,44,000
Other Allowances: Rs. 7,52,200

Taxable income after maximum deductions can be significantly reduced. The Rs. 1,60,000 NPS employer contribution alone saves Rs. 49,920 in 30% bracket + surcharge (if applicable).

## Provident Fund — Section 10(11) and Section 10(12)

Employee PF contribution up to 12% of Basic + DA: eligible for Section 80C deduction
Employer PF contribution up to 12% of Basic + DA: fully exempt under Section 10(12)
Interest on PF: Exempt up to Rs. 2,50,000 of employee contribution per year (Rs. 5,00,000 for government employees without employer contribution). Interest on employee contribution exceeding this threshold is taxable.

Note: If employer contributes more than Rs. 7,50,000 per year to PF + NPS + Superannuation combined, the excess is taxable in the employee's hands as perquisite.

## Choosing Between Old and New Tax Regime

For AY 2025-26, the New Tax Regime has the following slabs:
- Up to Rs. 3,00,000: Nil
- Rs. 3,00,001 to Rs. 7,00,000: 5%
- Rs. 7,00,001 to Rs. 10,00,000: 10%
- Rs. 10,00,001 to Rs. 12,00,000: 15%
- Rs. 12,00,001 to Rs. 15,00,000: 20%
- Above Rs. 15,00,000: 30%

Plus: Rebate under Section 87A up to Rs. 25,000 for income up to Rs. 7,00,000 under NTR (effectively zero tax up to Rs. 7L). Standard deduction of Rs. 75,000 under NTR means no tax up to Rs. 7,75,000 effective income.

Under the Old Regime:
- Up to Rs. 2,50,000: Nil
- Rs. 2,50,001 to Rs. 5,00,000: 5%
- Rs. 5,00,001 to Rs. 10,00,000: 20%
- Above Rs. 10,00,000: 30%

The Old Regime is beneficial when total deductions exceed the following break-even thresholds (approximate):
- For income up to Rs. 7.5L: Old regime rarely better
- For income Rs. 10L-15L: Old regime better if deductions exceed ~Rs. 3-3.5L
- For income Rs. 15L-25L: Old regime better if deductions exceed ~Rs. 4-4.5L
- For income above Rs. 25L: Old regime can save significantly with HRA + 80C + 80D + NPS

The best practice is to calculate tax under both regimes with actual numbers each year and choose accordingly. Employers must be informed of the regime choice at the beginning of the financial year.

## Summary of Key Sections

- Section 10(13A): HRA exemption
- Section 10(5) with Rule 6: LTA exemption
- Section 10(10AA): Leave encashment on retirement
- Section 10(11)/(12): PF exemption
- Section 80CCD(2): Employer NPS contribution deduction
- Section 80C: PF, ELSS, home loan principal, LIC premium (up to Rs. 1.5L)
- Section 80D: Health insurance premium
- Section 80CCD(1B): Additional NPS self-contribution (Rs. 50,000) — Old Regime only
- Section 17(2) read with Rule 3: Perquisite valuation rules
- Section 115BAC: New Tax Regime rules
