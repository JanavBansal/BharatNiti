INVESTMENT TAXATION AND STRATEGY IN INDIA — AY 2025-26 AND AY 2026-27
Income Tax Act, 1961 — Complete Guide for Investors

## Introduction to Investment Taxation

Investment taxation in India depends on four key variables:
1. The TYPE of investment (equity, debt, gold, real estate, etc.)
2. The HOLDING PERIOD (short-term vs. long-term)
3. The NATURE of gain (capital appreciation vs. dividend/interest income)
4. Whether the investment is structured for tax efficiency (EEE, EET, or fully taxable)

Budget 2024 (effective July 23, 2024) significantly revised capital gains tax rates and holding periods. This guide covers the updated provisions applicable for AY 2025-26 (FY 2024-25) and AY 2026-27 (FY 2025-26).

## Equity Shares and Equity-Oriented Mutual Funds

### Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) — Section 111A

For listed equity shares and equity-oriented mutual funds (where equity exposure is > 65%):
- Holding period for STCG: 12 months or less
- Tax rate: 20% flat (increased from 15% by Finance Act 2024, effective July 23, 2024)
- STT (Securities Transaction Tax) must have been paid on both purchase and sale (for listed shares on recognized stock exchanges)
- Basic exemption limit (Rs. 2.5L/3L/5L) is adjusted against regular income first; STCG at 20% is NOT reduced by the basic exemption

### Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) — Section 112A

For listed equity shares and equity-oriented mutual funds:
- Holding period for LTCG: More than 12 months
- Tax rate: 12.5% flat (increased from 10% by Finance Act 2024, effective July 23, 2024)
- Exemption: First Rs. 1,25,000 of LTCG per year is EXEMPT (increased from Rs. 1,00,000)
- Only gains above Rs. 1,25,000 are taxed at 12.5%
- No indexation benefit for equity — always without indexation
- Grandfathering: Gains up to January 31, 2018 (cost basis reset to price on Jan 31, 2018) are grandfathered for assets acquired before that date

### Rate Summary (Post July 23, 2024):
- STCG on listed equity/equity MF: 20%
- LTCG on listed equity/equity MF above Rs. 1.25L: 12.5%
- LTCG on unlisted equity shares: 12.5% without indexation (held > 24 months)
- STCG on unlisted equity shares: Slab rate

### Surcharge on Capital Gains:
LTCG under Section 112A and STCG under Section 111A: Maximum surcharge is 15%, regardless of the taxpayer's total income (surcharge on other income can go up to 37%). This cap at 15% benefits high-income investors with large capital gains on equity.

### Dividend Income from Equity:
Since FY 2020-21 (AY 2021-22), the Dividend Distribution Tax (DDT) has been abolished. Dividends are now taxable in the INVESTOR'S HANDS at their applicable slab rate (not at a flat rate). TDS under Section 194 is deducted at 10% if dividend exceeds Rs. 5,000 per company per financial year.

## Debt Mutual Funds — Post April 1, 2023 Change

### The April 2023 Amendment:
Finance Act 2023 removed the LTCG benefit for debt mutual funds. From April 1, 2023:
- All gains from debt mutual funds (regardless of holding period) are taxed at SLAB RATE
- No distinction between STCG and LTCG
- No indexation benefit
- The 20% with indexation LTCG tax on debt MF held > 36 months — abolished for funds bought on or after April 1, 2023

### Funds Bought BEFORE April 1, 2023:
Grandfathered under the old rules:
- Held > 36 months from purchase date: LTCG at 20% with indexation (old rule applicable)
- However, for such funds redeemed after July 23, 2024: Tax is 12.5% without indexation (new Budget 2024 rate applies)
Wait — per Finance Act 2024 clarification: For debt MF units purchased before April 1, 2023 and held > 36 months, LTCG is now taxed at 12.5% without indexation (Budget 2024 removed indexation as well). Consult a CA for unit-level computation.

### What Counts as Debt/Non-Equity Mutual Fund:
- Pure debt funds (liquid, overnight, ultra-short, short-term, medium-term, corporate bond, gilt)
- Hybrid funds with < 65% equity exposure (conservative hybrid, arbitrage schemes with < 65% equity)
- Fund of Funds (FoF) — taxed as debt unless investing in equity-oriented funds
- International funds (feeder funds investing abroad)

Tax efficiency of debt MF has significantly declined. Bank FDs, bonds, and PPF are often compared now.

## Public Provident Fund (PPF) — EEE Status

PPF is the gold standard for tax-efficient long-term savings, classified as EEE (Exempt-Exempt-Exempt):

- Contribution: Exempt — eligible for Section 80C deduction (within Rs. 1.5L limit)
- Interest earned: Exempt — Section 10(11) — currently 7.1% per annum, reviewed quarterly by government
- Maturity amount: Exempt — entire corpus including accumulated interest is tax-free
- Tenure: 15 years (can be extended in blocks of 5 years, each block with or without further contributions)
- Maximum contribution: Rs. 1,50,000 per year per person
- Minimum contribution: Rs. 500 per year (otherwise account goes dormant)
- Premature closure: Not allowed before 15 years except in specified circumstances (serious illness of account holder/family, higher education of account holder)
- Loan facility: Available from 3rd to 6th year against PPF balance
- Attachment: PPF balance is exempt from attachment by court orders (except Income Tax Department)

PPF is ideal for conservative investors who want zero-risk, long-term, tax-free compounding. The compound interest effect over 15-25 years is substantial.

## National Pension System (NPS) — Partial EEE

NPS is a market-linked pension product with the following tax treatment:

### Contributions:
- Section 80CCD(1): Employee/self-employed contribution — within Rs. 1.5L Section 80C limit
- Section 80CCD(1B): Additional contribution of up to Rs. 50,000 — OVER AND ABOVE 80C limit (exclusive deduction, only under Old Regime)
- Section 80CCD(2): Employer's contribution — up to 10% (private) or 14% (government) of Basic+DA, deductible with NO UPPER LIMIT — works in BOTH Old and New Regimes

### Returns:
NPS returns are market-linked (mix of equity, government bonds, corporate bonds depending on allocation choice). No guaranteed return. Long-term, equity-heavy NPS has historically delivered 10-13% CAGR.

### Withdrawal:
At age 60 or retirement:
- Minimum 40% of corpus must be used to purchase an annuity (pension for life)
- Remaining 60% can be withdrawn as lump sum
- Tax on withdrawal: The 60% lump sum withdrawal is EXEMPT from tax (Section 10(12A))
- The annuity (pension) received is taxable at slab rate as pension income

Partial withdrawal before 60:
- Allowed after 3 years in NPS for specified purposes (children's education, marriage, purchase of house, medical treatment)
- Maximum 25% of employee's own contributions
- Tax on partial withdrawal: Exempt up to 25% of employee contributions (Section 10(12B))

NPS status: Partially EEE (contributions deductible, returns accumulate tax-free, 60% withdrawal exempt; 40% annuity is taxable).

## ELSS (Equity-Linked Savings Scheme) — Section 80C with Equity Growth

ELSS is a type of equity mutual fund with:
- Section 80C deduction: Investment qualifies for 80C up to Rs. 1.5L
- Lock-in: 3 years (shortest among 80C options)
- Returns: Market-linked (equity mutual fund, typically 10-15% long-term CAGR)
- Taxation on gains: LTCG rules — 12.5% on gains above Rs. 1.25L (since held > 12 months by the time lock-in expires, it is always LTCG)
- Dividends: Taxable at slab rate

ELSS is ideal for investors who:
- Want Section 80C savings AND equity market exposure
- Can tolerate 3-year lock-in (vs. 15 years for PPF)
- Are comfortable with market risk

## Fixed Deposits — Fully Taxable

Bank Fixed Deposits and recurring deposits:
- Interest is taxable at the investor's applicable slab rate
- TDS under Section 194A is deducted at 10% when annual interest from a single bank exceeds:
  - Rs. 40,000 for non-senior citizens
  - Rs. 50,000 for senior citizens
- Senior citizens can submit Form 15H to avoid TDS

For high-income taxpayers (30% bracket), FD interest is taxed at 30%+cess, making effective post-tax return very low. Example: 7.5% FD returns 5.25% post-tax in 30% bracket (vs. PPF at 7.1% fully tax-free).

Alternative: Small Finance Bank FDs sometimes offer 8-9% interest. Tax treatment is the same, but pre-tax returns are higher.

## Dividend Income — Taxable at Slab Rate (Post FY 2020-21)

From FY 2020-21 (AY 2021-22), dividends from:
- Domestic companies
- Equity mutual funds
- Debt mutual funds (distribution of income)

are all taxable in the INVESTOR'S HANDS at the applicable slab rate. The DDT regime (where companies paid 20.56% DDT before distributing) was abolished.

TDS on dividend:
- Section 194: 10% TDS if dividend from a company exceeds Rs. 5,000 per year
- Section 194K: 10% TDS on income distributed by mutual funds

For high-income taxpayers, dividend stocks are less efficient than growth stocks (where returns are realized as capital gains at 12.5% LTCG rate vs. dividend at 30%+ slab).

## Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) — Most Tax-Efficient Gold Investment

SGBs are government securities denominated in grams of gold. They are issued by the RBI on behalf of the Government of India.

### Tax Treatment of SGBs:
1. Interest: 2.5% per annum paid semi-annually on face value — TAXABLE at slab rate. TDS is NOT deducted, but must be declared in ITR.

2. Capital Gains on Maturity (after 8 years): FULLY EXEMPT — Section 47(viic) specifically exempts any gain arising to an individual on redemption of SGBs from capital gains tax.

3. Capital Gains on Transfer Before Maturity (secondary market): If SGBs are transferred (sold on stock exchanges) before the 8-year tenure:
   - Held > 12 months: LTCG at 12.5% under Section 112A (since SGBs are listed securities)
   - Held ≤ 12 months: STCG at 20% under Section 111A

SGBs are highly tax-efficient for long-term gold investors who hold to maturity. The 2.5% interest is a bonus over physical gold or gold ETF, and the 8-year maturity exemption is unique.

Note: SGBs were last issued in 2023-24. As of 2025-26, there have been no new SGB tranches. Existing SGBs can still be traded on stock exchanges.

Comparison with Gold ETF:
- Gold ETF gains are taxable at 12.5% (LTCG if held > 12 months) — no maturity exemption
- SGB maturity gains: Fully exempt
- Physical gold: LTCG at 12.5% (post Budget 2024) if held > 24 months; 20% with indexation option for pre-July 2024 acquired gold

## LTCG Harvesting — Using the Rs. 1.25L Exemption

One of the most effective and legal tax strategies for equity investors is LTCG harvesting (also called "booking profits"):

### How It Works:
Each financial year, the first Rs. 1,25,000 of LTCG on listed equity/equity MF is EXEMPT from tax (Section 112A). If you have unrealized long-term gains and your portfolio gains have crossed this threshold:

1. Sell sufficient units/shares to realize exactly Rs. 1,25,000 in LTCG (gains above cost of acquisition)
2. Immediately repurchase the same shares/units at the current market price
3. The new cost basis is the current market price (higher than original)
4. Tax paid: ZERO (gains are within the Rs. 1.25L exemption)
5. Next year: Again harvest up to Rs. 1.25L

This effectively "resets" the cost of equity holdings at a higher level each year, reducing future taxable LTCG without any net change in portfolio holdings.

### Caveats:
- Not beneficial when unrealized LTCG is small (only do when gains > Rs. 1.25L threshold)
- STT is paid on both sell and buy — typically 0.1% on delivery trades; factor this in
- Brokerage costs on selling and rebuying — keep it minimal
- Best done near financial year end (March) after assessing total LTCG for the year

### Wash Sale Rules: INDIA HAS NO WASH SALE RULES. Unlike the USA, India does not restrict immediate repurchase after selling. The sell-and-rebuy strategy is entirely legal and widely practiced.

## Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP) Tax Treatment

SWP is a feature in mutual funds where the investor sets up automatic redemption of a fixed amount periodically (monthly, quarterly). It is popular among retirees as a replacement for pension.

### Tax Treatment of SWP:
Each SWP redemption is treated as a separate SALE of units (First-In First-Out or specific identification depending on fund house rules). Tax depends on:
- Units held > 12 months (equity MF): LTCG at 12.5% (if gains > Rs. 1.25L cumulative)
- Units held ≤ 12 months (equity MF): STCG at 20%
- Debt MF: Slab rate regardless of holding period

Since SWP redemptions spread over time, the holding period for each unit is tracked from when it was purchased (via SIP or lumpsum). In an SIP-SWP combination, the first SIP installment's units are redeemed first and have the longest holding period.

### SWP vs. Dividend Option:
- Dividend from MF: Taxable at slab rate (30%+ for high earners)
- SWP (LTCG from equity MF): 12.5% on the gain component only (not the full withdrawal)

In an SWP from an equity MF, only the GAIN portion is taxable — the principal component is returned tax-free. This is far more efficient than dividend payouts for high-income investors.

Example: Monthly SWP of Rs. 50,000 from an equity MF held for 5 years where NAV has doubled. Approximately 50% is return of principal (not taxable) and 50% is gains (LTCG at 12.5%). Effective tax rate on the SWP amount is approximately 6.25%.

## Tax-Efficient Investment Strategies

### Strategy 1: Pyramid by Tax Efficiency
Prioritize investments in this order (for same-risk category):
1. Employer NPS (80CCD(2)) — pre-tax, both regimes — fill first
2. PPF — EEE, risk-free, fill annually (Rs. 1.5L)
3. ELSS — 80C + equity growth; if 80C limit remains after PF/PPF
4. Direct equity/equity MF — 12.5% LTCG with Rs. 1.25L annual exemption
5. NPS self-contribution (80CCD(1B)) — only under Old Regime
6. Debt instruments (FD, debt MF) — highest tax burden; use last

### Strategy 2: Asset Allocation Across Tax Wrappers
- Hold debt investments in PPF (EEE wrapper) and long-term NPS (partially EEE)
- Hold equity in regular accounts where LTCG treatment is favorable
- Hold high-dividend stocks in tax-loss vehicles or within ELSS lock-in period

### Strategy 3: Harvest Losses Too
If you have realized or unrealized LOSSES in equity:
- Book losses to offset gains: Rs. 50,000 STCG from one fund offset by Rs. 50,000 STCL from another = zero tax
- STCL can offset STCG and LTCG; LTCL can offset only LTCG
- Losses can be carried forward for 8 assessment years if the return is filed on time

### Strategy 4: Regime-Specific Investment Focus
Under New Tax Regime:
- Most deductions removed — 80C, 80D, 80CCD(1B) not available
- But 80CCD(2) (employer NPS) IS available — maximize this
- LTCG harvesting (Rs. 1.25L exemption) still works — continue

Under Old Tax Regime:
- Maximize 80C (Rs. 1.5L): ELSS + PPF + ESIC/PF
- Add 80CCD(1B) NPS (Rs. 50,000 extra)
- Add 80D for health insurance
- Then apply capital gains optimization strategies

## Key Sections Reference

- Section 10(11): PPF interest exemption
- Section 10(12A): NPS lumpsum withdrawal exemption (60%)
- Section 10(12B): NPS partial withdrawal exemption (25%)
- Section 47(viic): SGB redemption exemption
- Section 80C: General savings deduction (Rs. 1.5L)
- Section 80CCD(1): Employee NPS contribution (within 80C)
- Section 80CCD(1B): Additional NPS self-contribution (Rs. 50,000, Old Regime only)
- Section 80CCD(2): Employer NPS contribution (no limit, both regimes)
- Section 111A: STCG on listed equity/equity MF (20%)
- Section 112: LTCG on other assets (12.5% without indexation post Budget 2024)
- Section 112A: LTCG on listed equity/equity MF above Rs. 1.25L (12.5%)
- Section 194: TDS on dividend from company (10% above Rs. 5,000)
- Section 194A: TDS on FD interest (10%, threshold Rs. 40K/50K for seniors)
- Section 194K: TDS on mutual fund income distributions (10%)
- Finance Act 2023: Debt MF LTCG benefit removed (post April 1, 2023 purchases)
- Finance Act 2024: LTCG rate changed to 12.5%; Rs. 1.25L exemption; STCG to 20%
