SUPPLEMENTARY KNOWLEDGE: CAPITAL GAINS TAXATION — COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE
Assessment Year 2025-26 and AY 2026-27 (incorporating Budget 2024 changes)

## Section 1: What Are Capital Gains?

Capital gains arise when a capital asset is transferred (sold, gifted, exchanged, or compulsorily acquired). The gain is the difference between the sale consideration and the cost of acquisition. Capital gains are taxed under the head "Capital Gains" under Sections 45 to 55A of the Income Tax Act, 1961.

Capital assets include: land, buildings, shares, mutual fund units, jewellery, paintings, bonds, debentures, and any other property of any kind. Personal movable property such as household furniture, wearing apparel, and motor cars used for personal use are not capital assets and gains on their transfer are not taxable.

## Section 2: Short-Term vs Long-Term Capital Assets — Holding Period

The classification into short-term or long-term depends on the period of holding:

### Equity Shares and Equity-Oriented Mutual Funds (listed):
- Held for MORE than 12 months: Long-Term Capital Asset
- Held for 12 months or LESS: Short-Term Capital Asset

### Unlisted Shares and Securities:
- Held for MORE than 24 months: Long-Term Capital Asset
- Held for 24 months or LESS: Short-Term Capital Asset

### Immovable Property (land, building, house property):
- Held for MORE than 24 months: Long-Term Capital Asset
- Held for 24 months or LESS: Short-Term Capital Asset

### Debt Mutual Funds (bought after April 1, 2023):
- Per Finance Act 2023: debt mutual funds bought after April 1, 2023 are ALWAYS short-term regardless of holding period. Gains are taxed at slab rates.
- Debt mutual funds bought before April 1, 2023: 36-month holding for long-term classification (transitional provisions apply).

### Other Assets (gold, jewellery, bonds, unlisted debentures):
- Held for MORE than 36 months: Long-Term Capital Asset
- Held for 36 months or LESS: Short-Term Capital Asset

### Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs):
- Redemption at maturity (after 8 years) is exempt from capital gains tax.
- Transfer before maturity: 12-month holding for LTCA classification.

## Section 3: Tax Rates on Capital Gains — Post Budget 2024

Budget 2024 (Finance Act 2024, effective July 23, 2024) significantly overhauled capital gains taxation. Here are the current rates:

### Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG):

**Section 112A — LTCG on Listed Equity Shares and Equity-Oriented Mutual Funds:**
- Rate: 12.5% (increased from 10% prior to Budget 2024)
- Exemption: First Rs 1,25,000 of LTCG in a financial year is exempt (increased from Rs 1,00,000)
- Tax applies on gains exceeding Rs 1,25,000
- No indexation benefit
- Securities Transaction Tax (STT) must have been paid on both purchase and sale

**Section 112 — LTCG on All Other Assets (property, gold, debt funds pre-April 2023, unlisted shares, etc.):**
- Rate: 12.5% (reduced from 20% prior to Budget 2024)
- Indexation benefit: REMOVED for assets transferred on or after July 23, 2024 (with exceptions noted below)
- Exception for property acquired before July 23, 2024: Taxpayers can compute tax as 12.5% without indexation OR as 20% with indexation, and choose whichever is lower. This grandfathering applies only to resident individuals and HUFs for property.
- For non-resident individuals: No indexation benefit at all, 12.5% flat.

### Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG):

**Section 111A — STCG on Listed Equity Shares and Equity-Oriented Mutual Funds (where STT is paid):**
- Rate: 20% (increased from 15% prior to Budget 2024, effective July 23, 2024)

**STCG on All Other Assets:**
- Taxed at the applicable slab rate of the taxpayer (same as regular income)
- This includes: short-term gains on property, debt mutual funds, gold, unlisted shares, etc.

### Surcharge on Capital Gains:
- LTCG under Section 112A: Surcharge capped at 15%
- All other LTCG: Surcharge at normal rates (up to 37% for income above Rs 5 crore in old regime; capped at 25% in new regime)

## Section 4: Computation of Capital Gains

### Full Value of Consideration:
The sale price received or accruing. For immovable property, if the stamp duty value exceeds the sale price by more than 10%, the stamp duty value is taken as the full value of consideration (Section 50C).

### Cost of Acquisition:
The original purchase price. For assets acquired before April 1, 2001, the taxpayer can use the Fair Market Value (FMV) as on April 1, 2001 as the cost of acquisition (since indexed cost calculations start from FY 2001-02 as base year).

### Indexed Cost of Acquisition (where applicable):
Indexed Cost = Cost of Acquisition × (CII of year of sale / CII of year of acquisition or 2001-02, whichever is later)

CII = Cost Inflation Index (notified by CBDT each year)

### Cost Inflation Index (CII) — Key Years (Base Year 2001-02 = 100):
- FY 2001-02: 100
- FY 2005-06: 117
- FY 2010-11: 167
- FY 2013-14: 220
- FY 2015-16: 254
- FY 2017-18: 272
- FY 2018-19: 280
- FY 2019-20: 289
- FY 2020-21: 301
- FY 2021-22: 317
- FY 2022-23: 331
- FY 2023-24: 348
- FY 2024-25: 363
- FY 2025-26: To be notified (expected around 376)

Note: CII is used ONLY where indexation benefit is available (which has been largely removed post-Budget 2024, except for the grandfathering provision on property for resident individuals/HUFs).

### Cost of Improvement:
Any capital expenditure incurred after the asset was acquired (e.g., renovation of a house). This is added to the cost of acquisition. Cost of improvement before April 1, 2001 is ignored.

### Transfer Expenses:
Brokerage, stamp duty paid by the buyer that is borne by the seller, legal fees directly related to the transfer — these can be deducted from the sale consideration.

## Section 5: Section 54 — Exemption on Sale of Residential House Property

Section 54 provides exemption from LTCG arising from the sale of a residential house property (used for residential purposes), if the proceeds are reinvested in another residential house property.

### Conditions:
- The asset sold must be a long-term capital asset.
- It must be a residential house property (not commercial property).
- The taxpayer (individual or HUF) must purchase a new residential house property in India within 1 year before or 2 years after the date of transfer, or construct a new residential house within 3 years.

### Amount of Exemption:
- If the new house costs more than or equal to the LTCG: The entire LTCG is exempt.
- If the new house costs less than the LTCG: Exemption equals the cost of the new house. The remaining LTCG is taxable.

### One House Limit:
With effect from AY 2020-21: A taxpayer can claim Section 54 exemption by purchasing/constructing ONE new residential house in India only. However, if the LTCG does not exceed Rs 2 crore, the taxpayer may invest in TWO residential houses. This option (two houses) can be exercised only once in a lifetime.

### Capital Gains Account Scheme (CGAS):
If the new house is not purchased/constructed before the ITR filing due date, the unutilized LTCG must be deposited in CGAS (a bank deposit scheme notified by the government). The amount must be used for purchase/construction within the stipulated period, failing which it becomes taxable in the year the three-year period expires.

### Lock-in:
The new house must not be sold within 3 years of purchase/construction. If sold earlier, the exemption is withdrawn and the LTCG becomes taxable in the year of sale.

## Section 6: Section 54EC — Exemption by Investing in Specified Bonds

Section 54EC exempts LTCG (from sale of any long-term capital asset) when the gains are invested in specified long-term infrastructure bonds issued by:
- National Highways Authority of India (NHAI)
- Rural Electrification Corporation (REC)
- Power Finance Corporation (PFC)
- Indian Railway Finance Corporation (IRFC)

### Conditions:
- Investment must be made within 6 months from the date of transfer.
- Maximum investment: Rs 50 lakh per financial year (combined across all specified bonds).
- Lock-in period: 5 years. If the bonds are sold, encashed, or used as collateral before 5 years, the exempted amount becomes taxable.
- The bonds are non-transferable and non-encashable before 5 years.

### Amount of Exemption:
Lower of: (a) LTCG amount, or (b) Amount invested in bonds (subject to Rs 50 lakh limit).

Note: Section 54EC applies to LTCG on any long-term capital asset, including property, gold, and unlisted shares — not just residential property. Interest earned on these bonds is taxable.

## Section 7: Section 54F — Exemption from LTCG on Any Asset (Other Than House Property)

Section 54F provides exemption from LTCG arising from the transfer of any long-term capital asset OTHER than a residential house property, if the net sale consideration is invested in a new residential house property.

### Key Difference from Section 54:
Under Section 54, the exemption is computed on the capital gains amount. Under Section 54F, the exemption is proportional to the net sale consideration invested (not just gains).

### Conditions:
- Asset sold: Any long-term capital asset other than residential house property (e.g., gold, unlisted shares, equity shares with LTCG above Rs 1.25 lakh, commercial property, etc.)
- The taxpayer (individual or HUF only) must not own more than one residential house (other than the new house) on the date of transfer.
- New residential house must be purchased within 1 year before or 2 years after, or constructed within 3 years.

### Amount of Exemption:
Exemption = LTCG × (Cost of new house / Net sale consideration)

If the full net sale consideration is invested, the entire LTCG is exempt.

### The CGAS and lock-in rules apply similar to Section 54.

## Section 8: Grandfathering for Equity Gains (Pre-2018 Positions)

When long-term capital gains on equity were reintroduced by Finance Act 2018, a grandfathering provision was included:
- Cost of acquisition for equity shares/equity mutual funds held as of January 31, 2018 is deemed to be the higher of: (a) actual cost, or (b) the lower of (i) FMV as on January 31, 2018 and (ii) the actual sale price.
- This ensures that gains accrued up to January 31, 2018 are not taxed.
- This grandfathering provision continues to apply even after Budget 2024 changes.

## Section 9: Set-off and Carry Forward of Capital Losses

- STCL (Short-Term Capital Loss) can be set off against both STCG and LTCG.
- LTCL (Long-Term Capital Loss) can be set off only against LTCG, not against STCG.
- If losses cannot be fully set off in the same year, they can be carried forward for 8 assessment years.
- Losses can be carried forward only if the ITR is filed on or before the due date.

## Section 10: Special Cases

### Inherited Property:
Capital gains tax applies on the eventual sale. The cost of acquisition to the previous owner (who bequeathed) is the cost of acquisition to the heir. Period of holding includes the predecessor's holding period. FMV as on April 1, 2001 can be used if the original owner acquired before that date.

### Gifted Property:
If received as a gift from a specified relative (as defined under Section 56(2)), the cost to the donor is the cost to the recipient. The period of holding includes the donor's holding period.

### Buyback of Shares:
As per Finance Act 2024, buyback proceeds in the hands of shareholders are now taxable as dividend income (previously tax-free). The cost of acquisition of the shares that were bought back can be claimed as a capital loss.

### REITs and InvITs:
Subject to specific provisions under Section 10(23FCA) and Section 10(23FC). Distributions can be in the nature of interest, dividend, repayment of debt, or capital gains, each taxed differently.
