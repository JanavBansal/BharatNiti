CRYPTO AND VIRTUAL DIGITAL ASSETS TAXATION IN INDIA — AY 2025-26 AND AY 2026-27

India introduced a dedicated tax framework for Virtual Digital Assets (VDAs) through the Finance Act 2022, effective from FY 2022-23 (AY 2023-24) onwards. This guide covers all aspects of crypto and VDA taxation under the Income Tax Act, 1961.

---

## Definition of Virtual Digital Assets — Section 2(47A)

Section 2(47A) was inserted into the Income Tax Act by the Finance Act 2022 to define VDA.

**A VDA includes:**
- Any information or code or number or token (not being Indian currency or any foreign currency) generated through cryptographic means or otherwise
- Providing a digital representation of value exchanged with or without consideration
- With the promise or representation of having inherent value, or functions as a store of value or a unit of account
- Including its use in any financial transaction or investment
- Non-fungible tokens (NFTs)
- Any other digital asset as notified by the Central Government

**Exclusions (by Government Notification):**
- Gift cards or vouchers
- Mileage points or loyalty cards
- Subscription to websites or online platforms or subscriptions to online games
- Any other asset specifically excluded by notification

The Central Government has the power to notify additional assets as VDAs or exclude assets from the definition. As of AY 2025-26, cryptocurrencies (Bitcoin, Ethereum, etc.), NFTs, utility tokens, and most digital tokens fall within the definition.

---

## Section 115BBH — Tax on Income from Transfer of VDA

Section 115BBH is the core provision governing the taxation of VDA income.

**Tax Rate:** 30% flat on income from transfer of VDA

**No Slab Rate Benefit:** The 30% rate applies regardless of the taxpayer's income level. Even if a taxpayer's total income is below the basic exemption limit, if VDA income is positive, it is taxed at 30%.

**Surcharge and Cess:** Health and Education Cess at 4% is always applicable. Surcharge applies based on total income:
- Total income ₹50 lakh to ₹1 crore: 10% surcharge on 115BBH tax
- Total income ₹1 crore to ₹2 crore: 15% surcharge
- Total income above ₹2 crore: 25% surcharge

**Effective Tax Rates:**
- Below ₹50L total: 30% × 1.04 = 31.2%
- ₹50L–₹1Cr: 30% × 1.10 × 1.04 = 34.32%
- ₹1Cr–₹2Cr: 30% × 1.15 × 1.04 = 35.88%
- Above ₹2Cr: 30% × 1.25 × 1.04 = 39%

---

## Allowable Deductions — Very Restricted

Unlike regular capital gains or business income, VDA income under Section 115BBH allows only one deduction:

**Only Deduction Allowed: Cost of Acquisition**

The taxpayer can deduct the amount paid to acquire the VDA — the purchase price. No other deduction is allowed, specifically:
- No deduction for cost of mining equipment, electricity used in mining
- No deduction for exchange fees, brokerage, or transaction costs
- No deduction for interest on loans taken to buy crypto
- No deduction for software, hardware, or custody costs
- No administrative expenses or overhead
- No transfer expenses (unlike normal capital gains where transfer expenses reduce the gain)

**FIFO Method for Cost Determination:** Where a taxpayer has purchased VDA of the same nature at different prices, the cost of acquisition is determined using the First-In-First-Out (FIFO) method (as per CBDT guidance and common interpretation, though not explicitly codified for VDA in all scenarios).

---

## No Set-Off of VDA Losses

**Key Restriction — Section 115BBH(2):**
- Loss from transfer of VDA cannot be set off against income from any other head of income
- Loss from one VDA cannot even be set off against profit from another VDA

**Example:**
- Bitcoin profit: ₹5 lakh
- Ethereum loss: ₹3 lakh
- Net VDA income: The ₹3 lakh Ethereum loss CANNOT be used to reduce Bitcoin profit
- Tax is payable on ₹5 lakh at 30%, not on ₹2 lakh

This is unique — even speculative business losses (equity intraday) can be set off against other speculative profits. VDA losses cannot be set off even against VDA profits from different coins.

---

## No Carry-Forward of VDA Losses

VDA losses cannot be carried forward to future years. Unlike:
- Business losses (8 years carry-forward)
- Capital losses (8 years carry-forward)
- Speculative losses (4 years carry-forward)

VDA losses are completely "dead" — they cannot reduce future VDA or any other income in subsequent years.

**Planning Implication:** There is no tax benefit in realizing VDA losses — unlike equity mutual funds or stocks where loss harvesting (tax loss harvesting) can reduce future capital gains tax.

---

## Section 194S — TDS on Transfer of VDA

Section 194S requires TDS at 1% on consideration paid for transfer of VDA.

**Who Must Deduct TDS:**
- Any person responsible for paying consideration for VDA transfer

**Threshold for TDS:**
- General taxpayers: If consideration in aggregate exceeds ₹10,000 during the financial year
- Specified persons (individuals/HUFs with business turnover up to ₹1 crore or professional receipts up to ₹50 lakh): ₹50,000 threshold

**Rate:** 1% of the gross consideration (not net profit)

**Timing:** TDS is deducted at the time of credit or payment, whichever is earlier.

**Exchange Obligation:** Crypto exchanges operating in India (regulated under PMLA 2023) are required to deduct TDS under Section 194S on behalf of sellers. The net amount (consideration minus 1% TDS) is credited to the seller.

**Foreign Exchanges:** If a resident transacts on a foreign exchange, the buyer (if Indian resident) or the exchange may have TDS obligations. In practice, for P2P transactions, the buyer (resident) must deduct TDS.

**TDS on Non-Cash Consideration (barter/exchange):** If VDA A is transferred in exchange for VDA B (crypto-to-crypto swap), TDS applies. The person paying (in kind) must:
- Ensure the TDS amount is deposited from own funds, or
- Ensure consideration is reduced to account for TDS

**CBDT Circular 13/2022 and 14/2022:** These provide detailed guidance on TDS under 194S, including provisions for exchanges and peer-to-peer transactions.

## Mining and Staking Income

**Crypto Mining:**
- Mining income is not taxable at the time of receiving mined crypto
- It is taxable only when the mined coins are sold/transferred
- At the time of sale: the full sale proceeds are taxable at 30% under Section 115BBH
- Cost of acquisition for mined coins: Nil (since no purchase cost) — the entire proceeds are taxable
- Mining equipment and electricity cannot be deducted as cost of acquisition

**Staking Rewards:**
- Staking rewards received are taxable as income from other sources under Section 56(2) at the time of receipt, at slab rates
- When subsequently sold, the cost of acquisition is the fair market value at the time they were taxed as income
- At the time of sale, any gain above this cost is again taxable at 30% under Section 115BBH
- This creates a dual tax event: receipt + eventual sale

**Validator/Node Operation:** Income from operating validator nodes is treated similarly to staking rewards — taxable as other income at slab rates on receipt.

---

## Airdrops

Airdrops (free distribution of VDA tokens) are taxable as income from other sources under Section 56(2)(x) upon receipt, valued at fair market value.

**Tax Treatment at Receipt:**
- If fair market value exceeds ₹50,000, taxable at slab rates as "income from other sources"
- The ₹50,000 threshold under Section 56(2)(x) applies to gifts received; airdrops are treated similarly
- Airdrops received as part of a hard fork may be treated differently (no acquisition cost, similar to mining)

**At Subsequent Sale:**
- The fair market value at time of receipt becomes the cost of acquisition
- Any further gain is taxable at 30% under Section 115BBH

---

## NFT Taxation

Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) are explicitly included in the VDA definition under Section 2(47A). However, the Central Government has the power to exclude certain NFTs.

**Transfer of NFT:**
- Profit taxed at 30% under Section 115BBH
- Cost of acquisition deductible
- No set-off or carry-forward of losses

**Creator of NFT:**
- Income from first sale of NFT created by the artist may be treated as business income or income from other sources (not 115BBH), depending on facts
- CBDT has not issued specific clarification; taxpayers should take a conservative position
- Subsequent sales of NFTs by collectors fall under 115BBH

---

## P2P and Decentralized Exchanges

**TDS Still Applies:** Even for peer-to-peer (P2P) transactions not routed through a centralized exchange, TDS obligations under Section 194S apply.

**In P2P Transactions:** The buyer of VDA is responsible for deducting TDS if the consideration exceeds the threshold.

**Decentralized Exchanges (DEX):** No clear mechanism for TDS compliance exists on DEXs. However, the legal obligation remains. Taxpayers using DEXs should maintain records and compute TDS liability manually.

**PMLA Registration:** Major crypto exchanges in India are required to register under the Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA) as reporting entities, enabling tracking of transactions.

## Record-Keeping Requirements

Given the strict tax treatment and potential for scrutiny, comprehensive record-keeping is essential:

**Records to Maintain:**
- Date and time of each transaction
- Nature of transaction (buy/sell/swap/stake/mine)
- Quantity of VDA transacted
- Consideration paid or received (in INR, converted at prevailing rate)
- Platform/exchange name and transaction ID
- TDS certificates (Form 16A) for TDS deducted by exchanges
- Wallet addresses involved in transactions
- Cost of acquisition for each batch of VDA (applying FIFO)
- Screenshot or PDF of trade history from each exchange

**Retention Period:** Tax records should be maintained for at least 6 years from the end of the Assessment Year to which they relate (to cover assessment and appeal periods).

**Exchange Downloads:** Most Indian exchanges (CoinDCX, WazirX, CoinSwitch, etc.) provide annual transaction statements for each financial year. Download and archive these immediately at year end.

## Gifts of VDA

**Section 56(2)(x):** Gifts received are taxable in the hands of the recipient if the total value of gifts from non-relatives exceeds ₹50,000 in a year.

**VDA Gifts:**
- If total VDA (and other gifts) received from non-relatives exceeds ₹50,000 in value, the entire amount (not just the excess) is taxable
- Taxed at slab rates as income from other sources
- Relatives (as defined under the Act) can gift without triggering this provision

**When VDA received as gift is later sold:**
- Cost of acquisition for the recipient = amount on which gift was taxed (i.e., fair market value at time of gift)
- If gift was fully exempt (from relative), cost of acquisition = fair market value at time of gift for subsequent VDA transfer tax purposes

---

## Reporting in Income Tax Return

**ITR-2:** For individuals with VDA income but no business income
- Schedule VDA in ITR-2 and ITR-3 (from AY 2023-24)

**ITR-3:** For individuals with business income in addition to VDA income

**Details Required in Schedule VDA:**
- Head of income (income from other sources or capital gains — for 115BBH)
- Date of acquisition
- Date of transfer
- Cost of acquisition
- Full value of consideration received
- Income chargeable to tax (before any deductions — only cost of acquisition allowed)

**Form 26AS and AIS:** TDS deducted under Section 194S by exchanges will appear in Form 26AS and Annual Information Statement (AIS). Taxpayers should reconcile these with their own records.

---

## Practical Compliance Tips

1. **Use Indian exchanges primarily:** They handle TDS (Section 194S) automatically. Foreign exchanges create compliance burden.

2. **Never mix wallets:** Use dedicated wallets for each type of activity (trading, staking, DeFi) to simplify record-keeping.

3. **Year-end download:** Download complete transaction history from every exchange before April 15 each year.

4. **Crypto-to-crypto swaps are taxable events:** Each swap is a disposal of the original VDA and acquisition of the new VDA. Maintain records of the INR value at time of each swap.

5. **No loss harvesting benefit:** Unlike equity, realizing crypto losses serves no tax purpose in India — they are permanently lost.

6. **Declare in AIS:** Cross-check AIS for any unreported VDA transactions that may appear based on TDS or bank transaction data.

7. **Foreign assets:** Holding VDA on foreign exchanges may trigger Schedule FA (Foreign Asset) reporting in ITR, depending on the nature of the asset. Consult a tax professional.
