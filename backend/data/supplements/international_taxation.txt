INTERNATIONAL TAXATION — INDIA AY 2025-26 AND AY 2026-27

This guide covers India's international tax framework including Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAAs), permanent establishment, transfer pricing, equalisation levy, and compliance requirements for cross-border transactions.

---

## Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAAs) — Overview

A DTAA is a bilateral treaty between two countries aimed at preventing the same income from being taxed twice — once in the country where income arises (source country) and once in the country of the taxpayer's residence.

India has DTAAs with over 90 countries as of 2025. These treaties are negotiated by the Government of India under the authority of Section 90 of the Income Tax Act.

**Priority of DTAAs:**
- Section 90(2) provides that the provisions of a DTAA override domestic law to the extent they are more beneficial to the taxpayer
- The taxpayer can choose to be governed by domestic law if it is more beneficial
- This principle — often called the "most beneficial" test — means taxpayers compare treaty rates with domestic TDS rates and apply the lower one

---

## Key DTAAs and Their Provisions

### India-USA DTAA

**Dividend withholding:** 25% (domestic) vs 15%/25% (treaty — 15% if recipient holds ≥10% voting shares, else 25%)
**Interest withholding:** 20% (domestic) vs 15% (treaty)
**Royalties/FTS:** 20% (domestic) vs 15% (treaty)
**Capital gains:** Generally taxed in source country (India) — US residents selling Indian property pay Indian capital gains tax
**PE article:** Permanent establishment triggers full business profit taxation in India

### India-UK DTAA

**Dividend:** 15% (if ≥25% voting stock held) or 15% in other cases
**Interest:** 15%
**Royalties:** 15%
**Capital gains:** India has right to tax capital gains from alienation of Indian property

### India-UAE DTAA

**Key feature:** UAE has no personal income tax. Indian residents who relocate to UAE and become UAE tax residents can benefit from this treaty.
**Dividend:** 10%
**Interest:** 12.5%
**Royalties:** 10%
**Capital gains:** Taxed in UAE (which has no CGT), but treaty benefits require genuine tax residency in UAE — not merely obtaining a UAE visa

### India-Singapore DTAA (As Amended)

**Pre-2017 era:** Singapore treaty had capital gains exemption; extensively used for FPI routing.
**Post-April 1, 2017:** Capital gains from Indian shares acquired after April 1, 2017 are taxable in India (grandfathering for pre-April 2017 acquisitions expired)
**Current Rates:** Dividends 15%, Interest 15%, Royalties 10%
**Principal Purpose Test (PPT):** The BEPS-aligned amendment restricts treaty benefits if the principal purpose of the arrangement was to obtain treaty benefits

### India-Mauritius DTAA (As Amended)

**Historical significance:** Mauritius was the largest FDI source into India historically due to capital gains exemption in the original treaty.
**Post-April 1, 2017:** Capital gains on shares acquired after April 1, 2017 are fully taxable in India
**Current Rates:** Dividends 5% (if ≥10% holding) or 15%, Interest 7.5%, Royalties 15%

## Section 90 and 90A — Claiming Treaty Relief

**Section 90:** Applies to DTAAs entered into by India with sovereign countries.

**Section 90A:** Applies to tax agreements between India and specified associations (not just countries).

**How to Claim Treaty Relief:**
1. Determine if a DTAA exists between India and the country of the non-resident
2. Obtain a Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) from the non-resident's country — this is mandatory (Section 90(4))
3. File Form 10F with the Indian tax authorities declaring TRC details
4. Apply treaty rate (if lower than domestic withholding) at the time of TDS deduction
5. File returns and claim treaty benefit if beneficial

**TRC Requirements:** The TRC must contain the non-resident's name, status, nationality, address, TIN in resident country, and period of validity. Without TRC, treaty benefit cannot be claimed.

---

## Tax Residency Certificate (TRC)

The TRC is a certificate issued by the tax authority of the country where the non-resident claims to be a tax resident.

**Mandatory from AY 2013-14:** Under Section 90(4), a non-resident must provide a TRC as proof of residence in the treaty country to claim DTAA benefits.

**Form 10F:** In addition to TRC, non-residents must file Form 10F if the TRC does not contain all required particulars. The information in Form 10F includes:
- Legal status of the taxpayer (individual, company, etc.)
- Permanent Account Number in India (if any)
- Address in India and in country of residence
- Period of residential status

**Online Filing:** CBDT has mandated online filing of Form 10F on the Indian income tax portal (income tax India e-filing) effective August 2023. Non-residents without a PAN may need to obtain a PAN for this purpose (or use the No-PAN option if eligible).

## Permanent Establishment (PE) Concept

The PE concept determines when a non-resident company's business profits are taxable in India.

**Definition:** A PE is a fixed place of business through which the business of an enterprise is wholly or partly carried on.

**Types of PE:**
- Fixed Place PE: Office, factory, branch, workshop, mine, oil well, etc.
- Construction PE: Building site, construction project lasting more than a defined period (typically 6–12 months depending on the DTAA)
- Service PE: Provision of services in India for more than a specified duration (typically 90–183 days in a 12-month period)
- Agency PE: A dependent agent with authority to conclude contracts, habitually exercising such authority
- Virtual PE (emerging concept): Significant economic presence without physical presence

**Significant Economic Presence (SEP) — Section 92F(iiia):**
Introduced from AY 2022-23 to address digital economy taxation:
- A non-resident has SEP in India if:
  - Aggregate payment received from India exceeds ₹2 crore (in a year), OR
  - It has 3 lakh users in India with systematic and continuous interaction
- SEP triggers business connection in India under Section 9 — but practical implications are limited by DTAAs (which may not recognize SEP-based PE)

## Transfer Pricing — Sections 92 to 92F

Transfer pricing rules prevent multinational groups from manipulating prices of intra-group transactions to shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions.

**Applicability:** Applies to "international transactions" between "associated enterprises."

**Associated Enterprises (Section 92A):** Companies are associated if one directly or indirectly holds ≥26% shares in the other, or one controls the other's Board of Directors, or they are both controlled by the same third party, among other conditions.

**International Transactions:** Any transaction between associated enterprises that may affect income, profits, or losses — including:
- Sale/purchase of goods
- Sale/purchase of intellectual property
- Provision of services
- Lending/borrowing (including intra-company loans)
- Cost sharing arrangements
- Business restructuring

**Arm's Length Price (ALP):** All international transactions must be priced at the arm's length price — the price that unrelated parties would agree to in comparable circumstances.

**Transfer Pricing Methods:**
- Comparable Uncontrolled Price (CUP): Direct comparison with independent transactions
- Cost Plus Method: Cost + arm's length markup
- Resale Price Method: Resale price minus arm's length gross margin
- Profit Split Method: Split profits based on contribution
- Transactional Net Margin Method (TNMM): Most commonly used — compare operating profit margins

**Form 3CEB:** Report on international transactions must be certified by a Chartered Accountant and filed by October 31 of the AY.

**Transfer Pricing Penalties:**
- Section 271(g): 2% of value of international transaction for failure to maintain/furnish documentation
- Section 271AA: 2% of value of international transaction for non-disclosure of required information
- Section 270A: 200% penalty for misreporting due to transfer pricing adjustment

## Equalisation Levy

**Background:** India introduced the Equalisation Levy to tax digital companies providing services in India without a physical presence.

**Two-Part Equalisation Levy:**

### 6% Equalisation Levy (Finance Act 2016):
- Applicable to: Digital advertising services provided by non-residents to Indian businesses
- Rate: 6% of consideration
- Threshold: Only applicable if annual payments exceed ₹1 lakh
- Who pays: The Indian business makes the payment and deducts 6% EL, depositing it with the government
- Exemption: Non-residents with a PE in India (they are subject to regular income tax instead)
- No income tax on such consideration for the non-resident

### 2% Equalisation Levy (Finance Act 2020):
- Originally applicable to: E-commerce supply or facilitation by non-resident ECOs to Indian buyers
- Rate: 2% of gross consideration
- **Finance Act 2025 Abolition:** The 2% equalisation levy on e-commerce operators has been abolished effective April 1, 2025. This was a significant change aligned with global OECD/Pillar One discussions. For AY 2025-26 and beyond, the 2% EL is no longer applicable.

**Current Status (AY 2025-26):**
- The 6% EL on digital advertising continues
- The 2% EL on e-commerce has been abolished

## Withholding Tax on Payments to Non-Residents — Section 195

When any person (resident or non-resident) makes a payment to a non-resident (that is chargeable to tax in India), TDS must be deducted under Section 195.

**Key Features:**
- No threshold limit (unlike TDS under 194J, 194C which have ₹30,000 thresholds)
- TDS applies from the first rupee
- Rate: Rates as per domestic law OR DTAA rate (whichever is more beneficial, provided TRC and Form 10F submitted)

**Common Payments and Default TDS Rates:**
- Interest payments: 20% plus surcharge and cess
- Royalties: 20% plus surcharge and cess
- Technical fees: 20% plus surcharge and cess
- Dividends: 20% (from AY 2021-22 onwards, post DDT abolition)
- Capital gains: As per applicable LTCG/STCG rates

**Application for Lower Deduction:**
- Section 195(2): Payer can apply to the Assessing Officer for determination of TDS on actual income (not gross payment), useful when payment includes significant capital component
- Section 195(3): Payee (non-resident) can apply for nil or lower withholding certificate

**15CA and 15CB Forms:**
- Before making any payment to non-resident that is chargeable to tax, Form 15CA must be filed online by the remitter
- Form 15CB is a Chartered Accountant's certificate (required for most remittances above ₹5 lakh)
- Form 15CA and 15CB are required before the bank allows the remittance

## Advance Ruling for Non-Residents — Section 245Q

Non-residents and foreign companies can apply for an Advance Ruling on tax implications of a proposed transaction in India.

**Benefit:** Provides certainty before undertaking a transaction — the ruling is binding on the assessee and the tax authorities.

**Forum:** The Board for Advance Rulings (BAR) — formerly the Authority for Advance Rulings (AAR).

**Application:** Form 34C (for non-residents) filed with prescribed fee.

**Typical Use Cases:**
- Whether a proposed joint venture creates a PE
- Whether royalty payments will be taxable under DTAA
- Whether a proposed restructuring is taxable as capital gains

## Black Money Act 2015 — Undisclosed Foreign Income and Assets

The Black Money (Undisclosed Foreign Income and Assets) and Imposition of Tax Act, 2015 is a standalone legislation targeting unreported foreign income and assets.

**Tax Rate:** 30% of undisclosed foreign income or the value of undisclosed foreign asset.

**Penalty:** 90% of the tax amount — effectively making total outgo 120% of the value (30% tax + 90% penalty).

**Prosecution:** The Act provides for rigorous imprisonment of 3–10 years for willful evasion of tax on foreign income.

**Disclosure Obligations for Indian Residents:**
- Schedule FA (Foreign Assets) in ITR must disclose:
  - Foreign bank accounts
  - Foreign financial interests (shares, equity in foreign companies)
  - Immovable property held abroad
  - Interest in foreign trusts
  - Any other foreign asset
- Every resident (not ordinarily resident and ordinarily resident both) must file Schedule FA if they hold any foreign asset

**Filing Requirement:** ITR with Schedule FA must be filed even if total income is below the taxable threshold — unlike regular ITR filing exemptions.

## Foreign Tax Credit — Section 90/91 and Rule 128

Indian tax residents earning income abroad may have paid taxes in the source country. They can claim credit for foreign taxes paid against Indian tax liability.

**Section 90 (DTAA Countries):** Credit for taxes paid abroad as per DTAA provisions.
**Section 91 (Non-DTAA Countries):** Unilateral credit available even without a DTAA.

**Rule 128 (Foreign Tax Credit Rules):**
- Credit available is the lower of: (a) foreign tax paid on foreign income, or (b) Indian tax payable on the same income
- Form 67 must be filed to claim Foreign Tax Credit — must be filed on or before the due date of ITR

**Example:** Indian resident earning $100,000 from US freelancing, US tax withheld at 30% = $30,000. India taxes the same income at 30% (assuming top bracket). Credit available = lower of $30,000 (foreign tax) and $30,000 (Indian tax on same income). Net additional Indian tax = nil.

## Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR) — Section 286

For large multinational groups with consolidated revenue exceeding ₹5,500 crore (approximately €750 million), Country-by-Country Reporting is mandatory.

**Who Files:** The Indian constituent entity of an international group must file the CbCR or notify the Tax Authority of the entity filing the master CbCR.

**Content:** Revenue, profit, employees, taxes paid, assets for each country of operation.

**Objective:** Allow tax authorities to assess transfer pricing risk and profit shifting.

## Non-Resident Taxation Quick Reference

| Income Type | Domestic Rate | Common Treaty Rate (varies by country) |
|------------|--------------|---------------------------------------|
| Dividends | 20% | 5%–15% (depending on shareholding %) |
| Interest | 20% | 7.5%–15% |
| Royalties | 20% | 10%–15% |
| Technical fees | 20% | 10%–15% |
| Capital gains (listed equity, >12 months) | 12.5% LTCG | Source country right varies by treaty |
| Capital gains (unlisted shares, >24 months) | 12.5% LTCG | Source country typically taxes |

All rates are before surcharge and cess. Apply the more beneficial of domestic law or DTAA provision.
