REAL ESTATE TAXATION AND TAX PLANNING — AY 2025-26 AND AY 2026-27
Income Tax Act, 1961 — Comprehensive Guide for Property Owners, Buyers, and Sellers

## Overview of Property Taxation in India

Real estate is one of the most significant asset classes for Indian taxpayers. The Income Tax Act deals with property income and transactions under multiple heads and sections:
- Income from house property (Section 22-27): Annual rental income and deemed income
- Capital gains (Sections 45-55A): Profit on sale of property
- Deductions for home loan (Sections 24, 80C, 80EE/80EEA): Interest and principal repayment
- TDS on property transactions (Section 194-IA): Buyer's obligation

This guide covers all aspects comprehensively for both self-occupied and let-out properties.

## Section 24(b) — Home Loan Interest Deduction

Section 24 allows deduction from income from house property for:
(a) Standard deduction: 30% of Net Annual Value (NAV)
(b) Interest on borrowed capital under Section 24(b)

### Self-Occupied Property:
- Maximum deduction: Rs. 2,00,000 per year
- This limit applies if the property is self-occupied and the loan was taken on or after April 1, 1999 for purchase/construction completed within 5 years (from end of FY in which loan was taken)
- If construction is NOT completed within 5 years: Limit is only Rs. 30,000
- The Annual Value (AV) of a self-occupied property is treated as NIL, so after interest deduction of Rs. 2L, the loss from house property is Rs. 2L maximum

### Let-Out Property:
- NO upper limit on interest deduction for let-out property under Section 24(b)
- The deduction is the ACTUAL interest paid on the loan
- NAV = Gross Annual Rent − Municipal Taxes Paid
- Section 24(a): 30% of NAV as standard deduction (no supporting bills needed)
- Section 24(b): Actual home loan interest (no ceiling)
- If NAV minus 30% minus actual interest results in a loss: The loss can be set off

### Set-Off of House Property Loss:
- Loss from house property (after interest) can be set off against other income heads (salary, business, etc.) in the same year — BUT only up to Rs. 2,00,000 per year
- Any remaining house property loss beyond Rs. 2L is carried forward for 8 years and can be set off against future house property income only

This Rs. 2L cap on set-off applies whether the property is self-occupied OR let-out. The change was introduced by Finance Act 2017.

### Pre-Construction Interest:
Interest paid during the period of construction (before the financial year in which construction is completed or possession is taken) is called pre-construction interest. This interest:
- Cannot be deducted during the construction period
- Is allowed in 5 EQUAL INSTALLMENTS starting from the year of completion/possession
- The Rs. 2L cap applies to the aggregate of current year interest plus the 1/5th installment of pre-construction interest

Example: Property purchased under construction in FY 2021-22; possession in FY 2023-24. Interest paid during FY 2021-22 to FY 2022-23 = Rs. 6,00,000 total. Annual installment = Rs. 1,20,000/year for 5 years starting FY 2023-24.

### Joint Home Loan:
Each co-borrower can claim the interest deduction proportionately to their share in the loan. Both must be co-owners. The Rs. 2L limit applies to each co-owner individually, so a jointly-owned property with joint loan allows Rs. 4L total deduction (Rs. 2L each).

## Section 80C — Home Loan Principal Repayment

The principal repayment on home loan is eligible for Section 80C deduction up to the overall limit of Rs. 1,50,000 per year.

Components included under Section 80C for home loans:
1. Principal repayment of the home loan
2. Stamp duty paid for purchase of property (in the year it is paid)
3. Registration charges for purchase of property (in the year paid)

Important conditions:
- Property must be from a notified housing finance company or bank
- If property is sold within 5 years of purchase, the deduction claimed under 80C in previous years is reversed and added back to income in the year of sale
- 80C for home loan is available ONLY under the Old Tax Regime

## Section 80EE and 80EEA — Additional Interest Deduction for First-Time Buyers

### Section 80EE (for loans sanctioned FY 2016-17):
- Additional Rs. 50,000 deduction on home loan interest over and above Section 24(b)
- Loan must be from a financial institution
- Loan value not exceeding Rs. 35 lakh
- Property value not exceeding Rs. 50 lakh
- Buyer should not own any other residential house at the time of loan sanction
- This section was for loans sanctioned between April 1, 2016 and March 31, 2017 — limited applicability now

### Section 80EEA (for loans sanctioned FY 2019-20 to FY 2021-22):
- Additional Rs. 1,50,000 deduction on home loan interest over and above Section 24(b)
- Loan must be sanctioned between April 1, 2019 and March 31, 2022 (budget extension)
- Stamp duty value of property must not exceed Rs. 45 lakh
- Buyer should not own any residential house on the date of loan sanction
- The buyer should not be eligible for Section 80EE (not simultaneously claimable)

Section 80EEA is still relevant for loans sanctioned in FY 2019-20, 2020-21, and 2021-22 as the deduction continues for the entire loan tenure.

Neither Section 80EE nor 80EEA is available under the New Tax Regime.

## Rental Income Computation — Net Annual Value and Standard Deduction

For a let-out property, income is computed as follows:

Step 1: Gross Annual Value (GAV) = Higher of:
- Actual rent received during the year
- Municipal Value or Fair Rent Value (whichever is higher) — known as expected rent or deemed rent

Vacant properties: If property is let-out for part of the year and vacant for remainder, actual rent received for the let-out period is used. The GAV is not inflated for the vacant period.

Step 2: Net Annual Value (NAV) = GAV minus Municipal Taxes paid during the year (must be paid by owner, not tenant)

Step 3: Standard Deduction under Section 24(a) = 30% of NAV (no supporting bills required — it covers repairs, maintenance, etc.)

Step 4: Home Loan Interest under Section 24(b) = Actual interest paid (no limit for let-out property)

Step 5: Net Income from House Property = NAV − 30% − Interest

Example:
Monthly rent = Rs. 30,000 → Annual rent received = Rs. 3,60,000
Municipal taxes paid = Rs. 15,000
NAV = 3,60,000 − 15,000 = Rs. 3,45,000
Standard deduction = 30% of 3,45,000 = Rs. 1,03,500
Loan interest = Rs. 2,50,000
Net income = 3,45,000 − 1,03,500 − 2,50,000 = NEGATIVE Rs. (8,500) — loss

This Rs. 8,500 loss can be set off against salary/other income (subject to Rs. 2L cap annually).

## Deemed Let-Out — Second Self-Occupied Property

Under Section 23(4) read with Section 23(1):
- Only ONE house property can be treated as self-occupied with Annual Value = NIL
- Any additional residential house owned (whether actually rented out or not) must be treated as if it is let-out for the entire year (deemed let-out)

For a deemed let-out property:
- Hypothetical rent is estimated (fair rent of similar properties in the locality)
- This hypothetical rent becomes the GAV
- Standard 30% deduction and home loan interest are still allowed
- The owner must pay tax on the "deemed" rental income even if no actual rent was received

From Finance Act 2019, the government allowed BOTH properties to be treated as self-occupied (nil Annual Value) if the owner uses both and has taken them for genuine reasons (job in different cities, etc.). Wait — this is NOT the current law; under the law two properties can have nil AV if the assessee chooses either. The second property the taxpayer cannot use due to employment being at another location can have nil AV. Consult a CA for specific scenarios.

Actually, the correct position: For AY 2019-20 onwards, the taxpayer can choose 2 houses to be self-occupied (the second being any house the owner declares). Both will have nil Annual Value. A third or subsequent house will be deemed let-out.

## Joint Ownership — Proportionate Deductions

When property is co-owned:
- Rental income is split in proportion to ownership shares
- Home loan interest and principal deductions are claimed by each co-owner in their respective proportions
- Each co-owner claims Section 24(b) up to Rs. 2L individually (for self-occupied property)
- Each co-owner claims 80C for principal repayment up to Rs. 1.5L individually

For maximum tax efficiency, co-ownership with spouse or family member is advisable when both are income-earners and home loan is taken jointly.

## Capital Gains on Property Sale

### Classification of Capital Asset (Section 2(42A)):
- Short-Term Capital Asset: Property held for 24 months or less
- Long-Term Capital Asset: Property held for more than 24 months

The holding period starts from the date of acquisition/allotment.

### Capital Gains Computation:

For Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG):

Method 1 — With Indexation (Cost Inflation Index):
LTCG = Sale Price − Indexed Cost of Acquisition − Indexed Cost of Improvement
Indexed Cost = Actual Cost × (CII of Year of Sale / CII of Year of Acquisition)
Tax rate: 20% on LTCG with indexation

Method 2 — Without Indexation (Post Budget 2024):
LTCG = Sale Price − Actual Cost of Acquisition
Tax rate: 12.5% on LTCG without indexation

Budget 2024 changes effective from July 23, 2024:
- Properties acquired BEFORE July 23, 2024: Taxpayer can CHOOSE the better of 20% with indexation or 12.5% without indexation. The one that results in lower tax is allowed (grandfathering provision)
- Properties acquired ON OR AFTER July 23, 2024: Only 12.5% without indexation is applicable

For Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG):
STCG = Sale Price − Cost of Acquisition − Cost of Improvement − Transfer Expenses
Tax rate: At applicable income tax slab rate

### Calculation Example (Pre-July 23, 2024 acquisition):
Property purchased in FY 2010-11 for Rs. 30,00,000. Sold in FY 2024-25 for Rs. 1,20,00,000.

CII for FY 2010-11 = 167; CII for FY 2024-25 = 363

Method 1 (20% with indexation):
Indexed Cost = 30L × (363/167) = Rs. 65,21,000 (approx.)
LTCG = 1,20,00,000 − 65,21,000 = Rs. 54,79,000
Tax @ 20% = Rs. 10,95,800

Method 2 (12.5% without indexation):
LTCG = 1,20,00,000 − 30,00,000 = Rs. 90,00,000
Tax @ 12.5% = Rs. 11,25,000

In this example, Method 1 (with indexation) is better by ~Rs. 29,000.

For longer-held properties, indexation benefit grows significantly. The taxpayer should calculate both methods.

## Section 54 — Reinvestment Exemption

Section 54 provides exemption from LTCG on sale of a residential property if the proceeds are reinvested in another residential property.

### Conditions:
1. The asset sold must be a LONG-TERM residential house property
2. The seller must be an individual or HUF (not a company)
3. The new residential property must be purchased within:
   - 1 year BEFORE the date of sale, OR
   - 2 years AFTER the date of sale
   OR
   Constructed within 3 years from the date of sale
4. The new property must be situated in India (no overseas property)
5. Maximum exemption: Amount of LTCG (not sale proceeds)
6. From AY 2020-21: Only ONE new residential property can be purchased (restriction added by Finance Act 2019)

### Capital Gains Account Scheme:
If the sale proceeds are not utilized by the return filing date (July 31), the unutilized portion must be deposited in a Capital Gains Account Scheme (CGAS) bank. This preserves the exemption while the property is being purchased/constructed. Withdrawal from CGAS must be for the specified purpose.

### Partial Reinvestment:
If investment < LTCG: Proportionate exemption
Taxable LTCG = LTCG − Amount invested in new property

### Lock-In:
The new property cannot be sold within 3 years of purchase. If sold within 3 years, the previously exempt capital gains become taxable in the year of sale.

## Section 54EC — Investment in Bonds

Section 54EC allows exemption from LTCG on sale of ANY long-term capital asset (not just property) if the LTCG amount is invested in specified long-term bonds.

### Specified Bonds:
- National Highways Authority of India (NHAI) bonds
- Rural Electrification Corporation (REC) bonds
- Power Finance Corporation (PFC) bonds (added later)
- Indian Railway Finance Corporation (IRFC) bonds

### Key Conditions:
- Investment must be made within 6 months from the date of transfer
- Maximum investment: Rs. 50,00,000 in a financial year
- Lock-in period: 5 years (extended from 3 years by Finance Act 2018)
- If bonds are sold/transferred/pledged before 5 years: Exempted capital gains become taxable

### Section 54EC vs Section 54:
- Section 54 is only for residential property sold → residential property bought
- Section 54EC applies to ANY long-term asset sold → bonds
- Section 54EC is simpler but limited to Rs. 50L
- Both exemptions can be claimed on the same property sale (Rs. 50L in bonds + additional in new property for the excess)

## Section 194-IA — TDS on Property Purchase (Buyer's Obligation)

When buying a property where the consideration exceeds Rs. 50,00,000, the buyer must:
- Deduct TDS at 1% of the total sale consideration at the time of payment
- This applies even if consideration is paid in installments — TDS is deducted on each payment

Who is responsible: The BUYER, not the seller. The buyer deducts and deposits TDS to the government.

How to pay:
- No TAN required for Section 194-IA (unique — other TDS requires TAN)
- Use Form 26QB — online on TIN-NSDL
- Due date: 30 days from end of month in which TDS is deducted
- Issuing Form 16B to seller within 15 days of due date of Form 26QB filing

TDS under Section 194-IA applies for Resident sellers only. For NRI sellers, the buyer must deduct TDS under Section 195 at higher rates (as discussed in the NRI taxation guide).

## Stamp Duty, Registration, and Their Tax Treatment

Stamp duty and registration charges paid on purchase of property:
- Deductible under Section 80C (within Rs. 1.5L overall limit)
- Deductible only in the year of payment

For capital gains computation: Stamp duty and registration are added to the cost of acquisition. When sold later, this increases the indexed cost, reducing LTCG.

Section 50C (Stamp Duty Value as Full Value of Consideration):
If the stamp duty value (circle rate) of a property exceeds the actual sale price:
- The stamp duty value is deemed to be the full value of consideration for capital gains computation
- Exception: If actual sale price is between 95-100% of stamp duty value, the actual price is used (relaxation introduced by Finance Act 2020 — raised to 110% for specified affordable housing)
- This provision prevents undervaluation/understating of property sale price

## Key Planning Strategies for Property Taxation

### Strategy 1: Combine Section 54 and 54EC
If LTCG is Rs. 80L, invest Rs. 50L in NHAI/REC bonds (Section 54EC) and Rs. 30L in a new residential property (Section 54) to achieve full exemption.

### Strategy 2: Joint Ownership for Maximum Deductions
Husband and wife as co-owners with joint loan: Each can claim Rs. 2L interest and Rs. 1.5L principal under their respective taxes.

### Strategy 3: Choose Indexation vs. Non-Indexation Wisely
For recently-acquired properties (post 2015), non-indexation at 12.5% may be better. For older properties with high original cost and long holding, indexation at 20% is often better.

### Strategy 4: Timing the Sale
Complete property sale in the same financial year as major deductible expenditure. For instance, if claiming 80-IAC (startup tax holiday), property LTCG is still taxable but coordinate with other income.
